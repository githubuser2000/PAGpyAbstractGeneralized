Angular Currency Economy

A complete model of an economy based on angles, power, and well-being

Status: Draft of an independent economic system
Core principle: Money amounts are lengths. Economic meaning is created by angles.
Goal: Not wealth as the final aim, but power-capacity and well-being.

1. Short definition

The Angular Currency Economy is an economic system in which markets are not primarily valued by numbers, but by directions. Three national governments form three political value circles. In each country, the government determines the good ↔︎ evil axis for buying, selling, and labor. Orthogonal to that lies the popular ↔︎ unpopular axis, which is not determined by the government, but by the population.

From these circles arise three currency vectors. These three vectors are three currencies. They may all be denominated in euro and may even have the same length. This means that 100 euros can have the same numerical length in all three currencies. They are still not the same currency, because their angles differ. They compete not primarily through exchange rates, but through direction inside the economic meaning-space.

The economy in this model does not seek maximum wealth. Wealth is stored length. The system seeks two higher aims:

  1. Power: the capacity of a currency to order markets, labor, contracts, and expectations in its direction.
  2. Well-being: the lived fit between population, work, consumption, social life, and economic direction.

2. What this system explicitly is not

This system is not a subsidy economy. It is also not a punishment economy. The terms good and evil do not mean “funded” or “punished.” They also do not simply mean “positive” or “negative.” They are poles of a political value circle.

A government does not merely say: “This receives money” or “This is penalized.” It sets an angle: this act of buying, selling, or working is closer to the pole good or closer to the pole evil. Independently, the population sets the orthogonal axis popular or unpopular. This creates four basic states that can all exist:

  • good and popular
  • good and unpopular
  • evil and popular
  • evil and unpopular

None of these states is automatically a price, a tax, a prohibition, or a reward. They are directions. The economy emerges from movement between these directions.

3. Basic elements

The system consists of the following elements.

3.1 Three countries

There are three countries, here called Country A, Country B, and Country C. Each country has:

  • a government,
  • a population,
  • a value circle,
  • an angular currency,
  • its own angles for buying, selling, and labor.

3.2 Three angular currencies

The three currencies are called here:

  • ΩA: the angular currency of Country A
  • ΩB: the angular currency of Country B
  • ΩC: the angular currency of Country C

All three may be denominated in euro. The euro is the shared unit of length. The actual currency, however, is not the length; it is the vector. One euro in ΩA, one euro in ΩB, and one euro in ΩC have the same accounting length, but different economic direction.

3.3 Three market actions

Each currency appears in three basic actions:

  1. Buying: What does it mean to receive something?
  2. Selling: What does it mean to give something away?
  3. Labor: What does it mean to create something through human activity?

These three actions are not identical. A good may be good to buy but not good to sell. A type of labor may be popular without being good. This makes the model more precise than an ordinary price economy.

4. Geometry of the value circle

Each country has a circle. In this circle lie two axes:

  • The good ↔︎ evil axis is determined by the government.
  • The popular ↔︎ unpopular axis is determined by the population.

These axes are orthogonal. Orthogonal means: one axis cannot be derived from the other. What is good does not have to be popular. What is popular does not have to be good. What is evil can be popular. What is unpopular can be good.

For technical notation, the poles may be written as circle directions:

  • good: 0°
  • popular: 90°
  • evil: 180°
  • unpopular: 270°

These degree marks are not value numbers. They are only a map. The value is not in the number, but in the direction.

5. Currency as vector

An angular currency is a vector in a value circle. It has two properties:

  1. Length: the money amount in the numerical market, for example euro.
  2. Angle: the economic direction in the value circle.

The length determines how much is paid. The angle determines what the payment means.

Example:

  • 100 euros in ΩA may lie near “good and unpopular” in a labor market.
  • 100 euros in ΩB may lie near “popular and evil” in the same act.
  • 100 euros in ΩC may lie near “good and popular.”

The number 100 is the same in all cases. The economic meaning is different.

6. The money-number currency market

Alongside the three angular currencies there is a money-number currency market. There, amounts, account balances, prices, debts, and payments are recorded as lengths. This market is necessary so that actors can calculate, book, and pay.

But the money-number currency market is not the highest reality of the system. It says only how long a vector is. It does not say where it points.

The classic error in this system is called length illusion. It appears when people believe that equal euro amounts have equal economic meaning. In the Angular Currency Economy, that is false. Equal length can be connected with opposing directions.

7. Markets of the system

7.1 Buying market

The buying market evaluates the receiving of things, services, rights, or relationships. The central question is: in which direction does a person or organization move when buying?

Buying can be good and unpopular, for example when necessary but unpleasant goods are bought. Buying can be evil and popular when something is desired while being close to the evil pole in the political value order.

7.2 Selling market

The selling market evaluates giving away. Selling is not merely the reverse side of buying. Giving away a good can have a different angle from receiving the same good.

A country may see the buying of a resource as good, but the selling of the same resource as evil because selling weakens its own power. Or a population may see the selling of a type of activity as unpopular, while the buying of its results is popular.

7.3 Labor market

The labor market is its own angular space. Labor is not only time exchanged for money. Labor is the human creation of world. Therefore labor receives its own direction.

A job can be good and unpopular. Another can be evil and popular. A third can be good and popular but poorly paid. The wage is only the length. Dignity, attachment, exhaustion, and social position arise through the angle.

7.4 Production market

Production is the transformation of angles. A firm receives labor, raw materials, knowledge, and money, then produces a good or service with a new angle. A firm is not strong merely because it reduces costs. It is strong when it can transform angles in a way that increases power and well-being.

7.5 Credit and future market

Credit is a wager on the stability of an angle over time. A lender expects not only repayment of length, but repayment in a compatible direction. The risk is that government, population, or market axes rotate so that the same repayment means something different later.

7.6 Foreign market

In the foreign market, the three countries meet. There ΩA, ΩB, and ΩC compete. Because all may be denominated in euro, the conflict is not always visible. It appears in acceptance, labor tension, contract stability, well-being, and shifts of power.

8. Actors

8.1 Government

The government determines the good-evil axis. It does not automatically set prices, prohibitions, or rewards. Its main function is the setting of political meaning: this kind of buying, selling, or working stands at this place in the value circle.

A government gains power when its angles are carried by markets. It loses power when its angles exist formally but are continuously bypassed by buying, selling, labor, and population.

8.2 Population

The population determines the popular-unpopular axis. This happens not only through voting, but through lived practice: demand, refusal, work choice, cultural recognition, habit, pride, shame, fatigue, and trust.

The population can make a government-defined good labor unpopular. It can make a government-defined evil good popular. This tension is not an error; it is a main engine of the system.

8.3 Households

Households seek well-being. They do not simply maximize income. They choose labor, consumption, belonging, and time so that life becomes bearable inside the value circle. A household may sacrifice length when doing so stabilizes its own angle.

8.4 Firms

Firms are angle transformers. They connect buying, selling, and labor. Their success depends on whether they can close cycles:

  • labor is received,
  • goods are produced,
  • sales are accepted,
  • purchasing power returns,
  • angular fracture remains low.

A firm may be rich and still weak if it accumulates length while its angles are not carried by population and government.

8.5 Banks

Banks are turntables between length, time, and angle. They manage not only money but also directional risk. A bank checks whether a loan can be repaid over time in the same currency, the same market action, and a compatible angle.

8.6 Clearing house

The clearing house maintains the angular ledger. It records not only amount, date, and debtor, but also:

  • angular currency used,
  • market action,
  • relevant value circle,
  • buying angle,
  • selling angle,
  • labor angle,
  • remaining rotational tension.

9. Price, value, and meaning

The price is the length of a vector. The value is its direction. Meaning arises from length and direction together, but direction has priority because the system does not seek wealth as its highest aim.

A complete price therefore does not merely say:

100 euros

but:

100 euros in ΩB, in the buying market, near good-unpopular, with selling angle evil-popular and labor angle good-popular.

This looks complex, but it is more precise. Classical prices hide this information. The Angular Currency Economy makes it visible.

10. Power

Power is the capacity of a currency to bind economic events in its direction. A currency has power when people, firms, and contracts voluntarily or habitually connect their angles with it.

Power appears in five forms:

  1. Market power: the currency is preferred in buying and selling.
  2. Labor power: labor is experienced as meaningful in this currency.
  3. Contract power: long-term obligations remain stable in this currency.
  4. Interpretive power: this currency’s angles shape what appears as good, evil, popular, or unpopular.
  5. Transformational power: the currency can absorb foreign angles without losing its own direction.

Power is not automatically oppression. But power without well-being becomes hard, brittle, and crisis-prone.

11. Well-being

Well-being is the lived fit between economic everyday life and the population axis. It arises when people do not constantly have to work, buy, or sell against their life-angle.

Well-being includes:

  • bearable labor,
  • understandable buying decisions,
  • social recognition,
  • low angular exhaustion,
  • trust in the currency,
  • the feeling that one’s life does not merely generate length but has direction.

Well-being is not the same as luxury. A rich system can have little well-being when its labor and consumption run against the population. A less rich system can have high well-being when its angles are stable and inhabitable.

12. Wealth

Wealth is stored length. It is useful, but it is not the highest goal. Wealth without power cannot guide. Wealth without well-being is felt as empty, foreign, or burdensome. Wealth without a fitting angle can even weaken the economy by attracting actions that twist the system inwardly.

In the Angular Currency Economy, wealth is therefore a tool. Power and well-being are the true target magnitudes.

13. Transaction rule

A transaction is complete when five statements are made:

  1. amount as length,
  2. angular currency used,
  3. market action: buying, selling, or labor,
  4. object or service concerned,
  5. angle in the relevant value circle.

A transaction is strong when buying angle, selling angle, labor angle, and currency angle form a closed circle. It is weak when it leaves high rotational tension behind.

Rotational tension means: the payment is completed, but the meaning of the action remains unresolved. Such tensions accumulate in firms, households, governments, and banks.

14. Accounting

Accounting has two levels.

14.1 Length ledger

The length ledger contains classical entries:

  • amount,
  • account,
  • receivable,
  • liability,
  • due date,
  • price.

14.2 Angular ledger

The angular ledger contains:

  • currency ΩA, ΩB, or ΩC,
  • country circle,
  • buying angle,
  • selling angle,
  • labor angle,
  • nearness to good, evil, popular, or unpopular,
  • open rotational tension,
  • expected future rotation.

A balance sheet is complete only when both ledgers are read together. A firm with a profit in the length ledger can make a loss in the angular ledger. Then it is rich but weaker. A firm with little length surplus may be strong in the angular ledger if it connects labor, sales, and population in a stable way.

15. Credit, interest, and debt

Debt is the promised return of a vector. The debtor receives length and angle today and must return length and angle later.

Interest in this system is not merely a reward for waiting. Interest is a premium for time rotation. The more government, population, or market axes are expected to rotate, the more demanding credit becomes.

There are three credit risks:

  1. Length risk: the amount cannot be repaid.
  2. Currency risk: repayment happens in another angular currency than expected.
  3. Directional risk: the amount is the same, but the angle has become foreign.

The third risk is especially important in this system.

16. Labor and dignity

Labor has its own part of the circle. It must not be reduced to wages. The wage is length. Dignity is angular integrity.

Work is dignified when the person does not have to work permanently against their own life-angle. Work can be highly paid and still undignified. Work can be poorly paid and still dignified. The system must not hide this distinction.

Unemployment does not only mean missing wages. It also means: a labor vector finds no place in the circle. The task of the economy is not only to create jobs, but to relate labor angles to buying, selling, and population angles.

17. Production and firm success

A firm processes four things:

  1. length,
  2. labor,
  3. materials or information,
  4. angles.

Classical efficiency asks: how much output arises from how little input? Angular economic efficiency additionally asks: how much power and well-being arise without destructive rotational tension?

Innovation here is not only new technology. Innovation is a new angular connection. An invention is strong when it connects formerly separated or contradictory angles into an inhabitable cycle.

18. Public budget without subsidy logic

The public budget is not the place where good automatically receives money and evil automatically loses money. That would return to the logic of funding and punishment.

In the angular model, the public budget is the material embodiment of the state’s value circle. The state buys, sells, and employs in the direction it has set as its own good-evil axis. Its expenditures show whether its angles can be inhabited. Its revenues show whether its population and markets can carry the circle.

Contributions to the public budget are not moral penalties. They are contributions to maintaining the shared circle: law, infrastructure, safety, clearing, education, health foundations, and public time order. Whether these contributions are experienced as bearable is decided by the population through the popular-unpopular axis.

19. Foreign trade between three angular currencies

Trade between the three countries does not occur only through quantities and prices. It occurs through angular translation.

An export from Country A to Country B carries the angle of ΩA. In Country B, that angle must be read, rotated, or rejected. Because both amounts may be denominated in euro, the accounting may look clean. But the angle may be foreign.

There are four types of foreign trade:

  1. Same-angle trade: both countries read the action similarly.
  2. Complementary trade: the angles differ, but they close a circle.
  3. Oblique trade: the angles fit only with mediation.
  4. Fracture trade: lengths are paid, but meaning breaks.

Fracture trade can create wealth in the short run and weaken power or well-being in the long run.

20. Competition of the three currencies

ΩA, ΩB, and ΩC do not compete merely through exchange rates. They compete through their ability to close economic circles.

An angular currency wins when:

  • it is understood in many markets,
  • it moves labor in a bearable direction,
  • it can connect buying and selling,
  • it remains trustworthy over time,
  • it can receive foreign angles without becoming empty.

An angular currency loses when:

  • it represents only length,
  • its good-evil axis is no longer inhabited by the population,
  • its labor angle becomes exhausted,
  • its contracts are constantly reinterpreted,
  • it builds power while destroying well-being.

21. Crises

21.1 Length illusion

Everyone sees equal euro amounts and believes everything is stable. In reality, the angles are drifting apart.

21.2 Good-popular split

The government places something near good, while the population places it near unpopular. This creates duty, fatigue, and resistance.

21.3 Evil-popular pull

The government places something near evil, while the population places it near popular. This creates temptation, bypass markets, and loss of trust.

21.4 Labor angle fracture

People work against their life-angle. Wages may rise, but dignity and well-being fall.

21.5 Power-well-being split

A currency becomes powerful, but life inside it becomes worse. Then a hard power body emerges without inner calm.

21.6 Well-being-without-power crisis

A population feels well, but its currency can no longer bind foreign trade, contracts, and labor. The country then becomes soft toward foreign angular currencies.

22. Stabilization

Stabilization does not mean making all angles the same. A living system needs angle differences. Stabilization means making angular fractures readable and bearable.

Important instruments are:

  • transparent angular labeling of every larger transaction,
  • separate recording of buying, selling, and labor angles,
  • regular population feedback on the popular-unpopular axis,
  • public angular balance sheets of firms and the state,
  • credit assessment by directional risk,
  • mediation markets for oblique foreign trade,
  • protection of labor dignity,
  • crisis protocols against length illusion.

These instruments are not subsidies and not punishments. They are instruments of readability and order.

23. Institutions

23.1 Angular registry

The angular registry contains the official angles for goods, services, rights, and labor types in each country.

23.2 Population council

The population council observes the popular-unpopular axis. It does not replace the population. It makes its movements visible.

23.3 Currency chamber

The currency chamber observes ΩA, ΩB, and ΩC. It ensures that equal euro length is not confused with equal angular meaning.

23.4 Clearing house

The clearing house connects the length ledger and the angular ledger. It shows where payments are finished but angular tensions remain open.

23.5 Labor dignity office

This office checks whether labor relations permanently run against the population’s labor angle. It does not evaluate people; it evaluates the fit of work inside the circle.

24. Contracts

A contract is complete when it names not only amount and performance, but also angle.

A complete contract contains:

  • currency ΩA, ΩB, or ΩC,
  • euro length,
  • buying angle,
  • selling angle,
  • labor angle,
  • duration,
  • expected possible rotations,
  • procedure in case of directional change.

This prevents two parties from meaning the same amount but expecting different economic meanings.

25. Property

Property is not only possession of a thing. Property is the capacity to hold the angle of a thing in one’s own circle. Whoever owns something but cannot stabilize its angle owns it weakly.

Strong property exists when purchase, use, labor, sale, and population form the same or compatible circles. Weak property exists when the thing is legally owned but socially, politically, or labor-wise runs against the circle.

26. Indicators without wealth fixation

This system needs indicators other than growth, assets, or gross domestic product. Important maps are:

  • Power map: which currency orders which markets?
  • Well-being map: where does the population live with or against the economic angle?
  • Labor angle map: which kinds of labor carry the circle, which break it?
  • Rotational tension map: where are payments complete but meanings open?
  • Foreign angle map: where do countries gain or lose direction through foreign currencies?
  • Length-angle map: where does the same euro amount hide different meanings?

In a simulation these indicators can be calculated with numbers. But the numbers are only measuring tools. The valuation remains angular.

27. Simulation schema

Each transaction can be stored as follows:

Transaction
- buyer actor
- seller actor
- labor involved
- object or service
- euro length
- angular currency: ΩA / ΩB / ΩC
- buying angle
- selling angle
- labor angle
- country circle involved
- rotational tension after settlement
- effect on power
- effect on well-being

Process:

  1. Determine action: buying, selling, labor, or combination.
  2. Select country circle.
  3. Select currency ΩA, ΩB, or ΩC.
  4. Read the action angle from the registry.
  5. Check population position: popular or unpopular.
  6. Pay the length price.
  7. Book angular tension.
  8. Update power and well-being maps.

Important: the calculation must not pretend that the angle is merely a hidden number. The number serves simulation; the model remains a model of direction.

28. Example

Three countries all use euro as length.

  • Country A places industrial labor near good, but the population experiences it as unpopular.
  • Country B places digital labor near good and the population experiences it as popular.
  • Country C places resource selling near good, but the population experiences it as unpopular because it makes the country dependent.

All pay 100 euros in wages. In the length ledger, wages are equal. In the angular ledger, they differ.

ΩB gains labor power because labor, population, and currency close a circle. ΩA carries duty tension. ΩC carries selling fracture. Externally, ΩC may look rich because resource exports bring length. Internally, it may become weaker because the selling angle runs against well-being.

This shows why equal money amounts create different economic strength.

29. Fundamental law of the Angular Currency Economy

An economy is strong when its currencies are not only long, but direction-capable.

An economy is inhabitable when its directions are not only powerful, but bearable for the population.

An economy breaks apart when it confuses equal length with equal meaning.

30. Compact formula in words

Money amount is length. Currency is angle. Market is movement. Government sets good and evil. Population sets popular and unpopular. Buying, selling, and labor create three directional fields. Three countries create three competing angular currencies. Economic strength does not arise from wealth alone, but from the capacity to hold power and well-being in a closed circle.

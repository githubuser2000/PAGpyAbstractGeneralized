# Galactic Metric Operator Economy  
## A Complete Economic System Based on Unary Algebra, Metrics, and Certified State Transformation

### Status

This document specifies a complete conceptual economic system in which payment is not primarily made with ordinary value, but with **unary operations plus metrics**: a participant pays by performing, owning, transferring, or guaranteeing a measurable transformation of the world-state.

The system is designed to make noble, helpful, agency-increasing action economically liquid, while making harmful, degrading, helplessness-producing action costly, liable, or non-payable.

---

# 0. Conversation History and Origin of the Model

## 0.1 Initial question: algebra of unary operations

The starting point was the question whether there is an algebra of unary operations.

A unary operation is a map

\[
f:A\to A
\]

on a set \(A\). A structure \((A,f)\) is a unary or monounary algebra. The set of all unary operations on \(A\),

\[
A^A=\{f:A\to A\},
\]

forms a monoid under composition:

\[
(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x)).
\]

This is the full transformation monoid.

A single unary operation can also be viewed dynamically: repeated application gives

\[
f^0=\operatorname{id},\quad f^1=f,\quad f^2=f\circ f,\ldots
\]

So a unary operation is also a theory of iteration and state change.

## 0.2 Hierarchy inversion

The next step was to interpret unary operations as transformations of hierarchies.

A hierarchy can be modeled as a state

\[
s=(A,\leq,r,P,L)
\]

where:

- \(A\) is the set of actors,
- \(\leq\) is a hierarchy relation,
- \(r:A\to\mathbb Z\) is a rank function,
- \(P\) is a permission structure,
- \(L\) is a ledger or history.

A unary operation

\[
u:S\to S
\]

can promote, demote, invert, override, or repair a hierarchy.

The idea emerged that such operations could become economically tradable: one could buy or sell the right to perform state transformations.

## 0.3 Galaxy-wide economy

The model was then generalized from hierarchy alone to a galaxy-wide economy involving:

- ordinary money from numbers,
- securities, certificates, stocks,
- money from truth values,
- causal intervention rights,
- determinations, effects, potentials, intensities,
- money from distances between norms,
- behavior-purchasing mechanisms,
- invertible positive and negative positions.

This required metrics. Without metrics, the system cannot know whether a transformation is good, harmful, strong, weak, reversible, costly, or agency-increasing.

## 0.4 Market exchange alone is insufficient

A normal market does not automatically reward noble action. It rewards what is priced, enforceable, and profitable. Therefore the system must not merely permit exchange; it must define valid payment as a **certified metric improvement**.

## 0.5 Key correction: payment is made with algebra and metric

The decisive correction was:

Participants do not pay with bare value.

They pay with:

\[
\text{unary operation}+\text{metric}+\text{certified state change}.
\]

So payment is not merely a transfer of stored wealth. Payment is the delivery, transfer, or guarantee of a measurable transformation.

The core formula becomes:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Payment}=\text{certified reduction of a valid distance by a valid operation}.
}
\]

\[
\Delta_d(f,\omega)

\[
\Delta_d(f,\omega)>0,
\]

the operation reduced the relevant distance.

If

\[
\Delta_d(f,\omega)<0,

the operation increased the relevant distance and therefore creates liability, debt, or negative reputation.

\[
\mathfrak G

Invertible arrows form a groupoid:

\[
\mathfrak G^\times.
\]

Non-invertible arrows remain valid but require liability, audit, or repair rules.

# 7. Payment

A payment is not fundamentally a scalar transfer.

A payment is:

\[
\kappa=(a,b,f,\vec d,\omega,\omega',\Delta,\Pi,\sigma,J,t)
\]

where:

- \(a\) is payer,
- \(b\) is receiver,
- \(f\) is the operation,
- \(\vec d\) is the metric vector,
- \(\omega\) is the pre-state,
- \(\omega'=f(\omega)\) is the post-state,
- \(\Delta\) is the measured improvement vector,
- \(\Pi\) is proof or audit evidence,
- \(\sigma\) is stake or liability,
- \(J\) is jurisdiction,
- \(t\) is timestamp.

The improvement vector is:

\[
\Delta(f,\omega)
=
\vec d(\omega,\omega^\*)-\vec d(f(\omega),\omega^\*).
\]

A payment is positive if:

\[
\Delta(f,\omega)\in K_+
\]

where \(K_+\) is the positive cone of valid improvement directions.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Valid payment}=\text{valid operation}+\text{valid metric}+\text{positive certified distance change}.
}
\]

---

# 8. The Positive Cone

The positive cone

\[
K_+
\]

defines which metric changes count as positive.

An operation may improve one metric but damage another. For example:

- productivity may rise,
- but agency may fall.

The system does not allow a single improvement to hide serious degradation.

Therefore:

\[
\Delta\in K_+
\]

only if:

1. agency does not fall below protected thresholds,
2. harm does not increase beyond allowed limits,
3. truth is not degraded by fraud,
4. consent constraints are satisfied,
5. rights are not violated,
6. irreversible damage is either absent or explicitly justified under emergency law,
7. affected parties are represented.

A typical rule:

\[
\Delta_A\geq 0
\]

\[
\Delta_D\geq 0
\]

\[
\Delta_T\geq 0
\]

with possible bounded exceptions only under strict emergency conditions.

Here \(\Delta_D\geq 0\) means damage distance is reduced.

# 9. Operation-Metric Credits

An Operation-Metric Credit is a tradable unit representing certified or guaranteed metric improvement.

\[
\text{OMC}=(f,\vec d,\Delta,\Pi,\sigma,J,\tau)
\]

where \(\tau\) defines duration, maturity, or validity window.

There are three major forms.

## 9.1 Realized OMC

A realized OMC represents an improvement already performed and verified.

\[
\omega' = f(\omega)
\]

\[
\Delta\in K_+
\]

## 9.2 Forward OMC

A forward OMC promises future improvement.

\[
\mathbb E[\Delta]\in K_+
\]

The issuer must stake collateral.

## 9.3 Option OMC

An option OMC grants the right, but not obligation, to execute an operation under conditions.

\[
\text{Right}(f,C,t)
\]

where \(C\) is an execution condition.

# 10. Ordinary Money as a Special Case

Ordinary money is a scalar representation of OMCs.

A pricing functional

\[
P:K_+\to\mathbb R_{\geq 0}
\]

maps improvement vectors into scalar units.

\[
m=P(\Delta).
\]

This creates number-money.

But number-money is derivative.

The root money is not the number. The root money is certified transformation.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{ordinary money}=\text{scalarized operator-metric credit}.
}
\]

A payment of ordinary money is valid only because it ultimately corresponds to:

- past verified improvement,
- future guaranteed improvement,
- accepted reserve credit,
- or legally recognized claim on transformation capacity.

# 11. Monetary Supply

New scalar money is minted when valid OMCs are created.

\[
\Delta\in K_+\Rightarrow \text{mint }P(\Delta)
\]

Money is burned when:

- promises fail,
- damage is caused,
- credit is reversed,
- fraud is discovered,
- liabilities are settled.

\[
\Delta\notin K_+\Rightarrow \text{burn, slash, or charge liability}.
\]

Inflation control is achieved by anchoring issuance to verified metric improvement, not arbitrary expansion.

# 18. Hierarchy Money and Hierarchy Inversion

A hierarchy can be represented by:

\[
r:A\to\mathbb Z
\]

or by a partial order:

\[
(A,\leq).
\]

A local inversion over a subset \(B\subseteq A\) is:

\[
r'(x)=\max_B r+\min_B r-r(x)
\]

for \(x\in B\), and

\[
r'(x)=r(x)
\]

for \(x\notin B\).

This flips local hierarchy.

A hierarchy inversion credit is valid only if:

\[
\Delta_H\in K_+
\]

and agency, rights, and damage constraints are respected.

Hierarchy inversion can be used to:

- remove corrupt leadership,
- rotate authority,
- restore dignity,
- decentralize command,
- reverse unjust dominance,
- give lower-ranked actors temporary override power.

But it cannot be used merely to replace one domination structure with another.

\[

\]

satisfies:

\[
f^{-1}(f(\omega))=\omega
\]

and

\[
f(f^{-1}(\omega))=\omega.

## 20.2 Financial inverse

\[

inverts payoff exposure.

Example:

## 20.3 Repair inverse

\[
f^\star
\]

## 20.4 Semantic negation

\[
P\mapsto \neg P
\]

inverts proposition truth conditions.

This does not change reality; it changes claim exposure.

Core rule:

\[
\boxed{
\text{Harm may be repaired, hedged, or prevented, but not relabeled as help by payment.}

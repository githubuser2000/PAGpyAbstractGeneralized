# Reality-Cuboid Planetary Economy

## An economy of number, truth, and volume

This document describes a planetary economic system in which money carries not only a numerical amount but also a tradable truth value. The truth value is **not primarily a legal tool for later challenging a trade**. It is a core component of the trade itself, just like the number. Two market participants agree on a number, a truth value, and therefore on a volume.

The basic object is:

\[
Q = (S, z, f, h, d, t, r)
\]

Where:

- `S` is the statement, object, determination, hypothesis, effect, or phenomenon being traded.
- `z` is the numerical value, price, amount, cost value, or utility value.
- `f` is the truth value from `-1` to `+1`.
- `h` is the stack height: quantity, reach, repeatability, or weighting.
- `d` is the dimension: matter, time, causality, space, impact, randomness, and so on.
- `t` is the time reference.
- `r` is evidential strength, measurement quality, or reliability.

The volume is:

\[
V = z \cdot f \cdot h
\]

The signs of the number and the truth value are stored separately:

\[
\operatorname{sgn}(z), \operatorname{sgn}(f)
\]

This is necessary because `(+z)(+f)` is not the same as `(-z)(-f)`, even if both can yield a positive product. Likewise, `(+z)(-f)` is not the same as `(-z)(+f)`, even if both can yield a negative product.

---

## 1. The decisive difference from normal money

Normal money asks only: “How much?”

Reality-cuboid money also asks:

- How real is the thing?
- Is the statement verified or falsified?
- Does the claimed state exist?
- What effect actually occurs?
- How intense is the effect?
- In which space and time does the statement hold?
- Which causality is supported?
- Which possibility is realistic?
- Which non-existence or non-effect has been proven?

This means that the currency does not only buy goods and services. It buys **determined reality**. It does not buy truth in a metaphysical sense. It buys tradable, testable reality-statements.

Examples:

- “This material contains 4.2 percent nickel.”
- “This machine reduces energy consumption by 17 percent.”
- “This supply chain actually exists.”
- “This medical treatment does not work in this patient group.”
- “There is no robust causal relation between A and B.”
- “This risk exists in region X with probability Y.”

Falsification is valuable. A negative truth value does not automatically mean fraud. It can mean that a claim is strongly refuted, an effect does not occur, a cause is excluded, or an existence claim is negated.

## 2. The four basic quadrants

| Number | Truth value | Volume direction | Meaning |
|---:|---:|---:|---|
| `+z` | `+f` | positive | positive value of a verified statement, existence, effect, or object |
| `+z` | `-f` | negative | positive price for falsification, non-effect, non-existence, or counter-truth |
| `-z` | `+f` | negative | real costs, real burden, real negative effect, or real consumption |
| `-z` | `-f` | positive | negative amount linked to a falsified burden, avoided cost, or cancelled negative effect |

The second quadrant is especially important. It shows that a market can pay for refutations. Example:

> A firm pays 2 million planetary units for proof that its previous technical assumption is false.

Then:

\[
z = +2{,}000{,}000, \quad f = -0.9
\]

The volume is negative relative to the original claim, but economically valuable because the falsification prevents misinvestment.

## 3. Tradable categories of reality

With this money one can buy and sell phenomena, determinations, discoveries, comparisons, existences, presence, time, causality, intensity, directions, decisions, space, potentials, effects, differences, substance, matter, unipolarity, coincidences, transformations, randomness, attractive forces, maximizations, minimizations, and impacts.

These categories are not merely philosophical. They become economic goods.

### 3.1 Phenomena

A phenomenon is an observable event or pattern: a disease cluster, a material effect, a technical fault, a market behavior, a weather event, or a social reaction.

\[
Q_{phenomenon} = (\text{phenomenon description}, z, f, h)
\]

Value arises from observability, rarity, economic importance, repeatability, and decision usefulness.

### 3.2 Determinations

Determinations are classifications: Is a substance hazardous? Is a payment income? Is a machine functional? Does a symptom belong to a disease?

Determinations order markets. Without determination, trade is uncertain. Therefore determination itself becomes tradable.

### 3.3 Discoveries

A discovery is newly found knowledge. It can be positive discovery or negative discovery, meaning the refutation of an error. Both can create value.

### 3.4 Comparisons

Comparisons are relations: A is faster than B, product X saves more energy than product Y, country A uses resources more efficiently than country B.

Markets need comparisons because purchase decisions depend on distinctions.

### 3.5 Existence and presence

The statement that something exists or does not exist becomes tradable: a resource deposit, a risk, a demand, a buyer, a disease, a patent obstacle.

Non-existence can be valuable. Proving that a risk is absent can lower insurance premiums and enable investment.

### 3.6 Time

Time is not only labor time. It is validity, actuality, sequence, duration, decay, latency, and future horizon. Early truth is often more valuable than late truth.

\[
V = z \cdot f \cdot h \cdot \theta(t)
\]

`θ(t)` is the time factor.

### 3.7 Causality

Causality is one of the most valuable trading classes. Whoever proves that A actually causes B owns causal capital. Whoever proves that A does not cause B creates falsification capital.

### 3.8 Intensity

Intensity describes strength, degree, density, or magnitude. Not merely “the effect exists,” but “the effect exists with strength 0.63.”

### 3.9 Directions and decisions

Directions describe development paths. Decisions are actions with claimed effects. A political decision, investment, or technical control is valued as a causal and impact cuboid.

### 3.10 Space

Space includes location, area, distance, access, reach, boundary, property space, digital space, production space, and ecological space.

A plot of land is not only area. It is a space cuboid composed of soil quality, climate risk, use potential, infrastructure access, and time development.

### 3.11 Potentials and effects

Potential is a not-yet-realized possibility. Effect is an actual or expected consequence. Investments trade potential cuboids.

### 3.12 Differences

Differences are economically valuable because innovation and decision arise from differences. The difference between two technologies can itself have a market value.

### 3.13 Substance and matter

Matter is traded not only by quantity but by composition, purity, energy content, stability, origin, recyclability, and transformability.

### 3.14 Unipolarity

Unipolarity describes dominant poles: monopolies, one-sided dependencies, bottlenecks, platform power, or a single critical supplier.

### 3.15 Coincidences

Coincidence is the joint occurrence of events. The important point is to separate coincidence from causality. That A and B occur together does not mean that A causes B.

### 3.16 Transformation

Transformation means converting one state into another: raw material into product, data into insight, education into competence, waste into resource, capital into infrastructure.

### 3.17 Randomness

Randomness becomes tradable as a probability structure: failure risk, weather risk, disease risk, market fluctuation, supply-chain risk.

### 3.18 Attractive forces

Attractive forces are pull effects: demand pulls supply, wages pull labor, return pulls capital, trust pulls investment, platforms pull users.

### 3.19 Maximizations and minimizations

Optimization becomes tradable: minimize costs, maximize impact, minimize risk, reduce energy use, improve health, reduce emissions.

### 3.20 Impacts

Impacts are consequences of actions: environmental impact, social impact, health effect, productivity effect, educational effect, geopolitical effect.

## 4. Market orders in the reality-cuboid system

A market order is no longer only:

> Buy 1 ton of steel for 800.

It becomes:

```text
Buy:
Object: 1 ton of steel
Dimensions: matter, origin, time, impact, transformability
Number: +800 planetary units
Truth values: material 0.96; origin 0.88; CO₂ impact 0.74; delivery time 0.91
Evidence standard: laboratory and supply-chain proof
```

For knowledge:

```text
Buy:
Statement: cause A produces error B
Category: causality
Number: +50,000
Truth value: at least +0.80
Validity: factory X, process Y, period Z
```

For falsification:

```text
Buy:
Statement: hypothesis H is false
Category: falsification
Number: +80,000
Truth direction: -0.90 or stronger
```

The market therefore trades not only things, but statements about things.

## 5. Planetary currency

All countries use one planetary currency while keeping national representations. A national currency is not a completely separate money-world; it is a national coordinate of the planetary currency.

\[
W_k = c_k \cdot P
\]

Where:

- `W_k` is the national representation in country `k`.
- `P` is the planetary value.
- `c_k` is the country coefficient.

The country coefficient includes purchasing power, price level, productivity, resource consumption, institutional stability, education, data quality, and average truth volume.

A country is therefore valued not only by GDP and exchange rates, but by its planetary reality volume.

---

## 6. Valuing countries

A country has several volume layers:

1. material volume: resources, infrastructure, energy, buildings, soils, water.
2. knowledge volume: education, research, data quality, scientific insight.
3. causal volume: ability to determine cause and effect correctly.
4. decision volume: ability to make political and economic decisions that actually work.
5. impact volume: positive and negative effects on environment, society, and the world economy.
6. time volume: speed of recognition, reaction, correction, and transformation.
7. trust volume: quality of institutions, measurements, and markets.

A country with high GDP but weak truth, poor education, and strong environmental damage may look rich in the old system but unstable in the new one. A smaller country with strong education, high measurement quality, and good transformation capacity may be more valuable planetarily.

---

## 7. Valuing companies

A company is not valued only by revenue, profit, and market capitalization. It receives a reality balance sheet:

\[
U_Q = \sum_i z_i \cdot f_i \cdot h_i
\]

Key areas:

- product truth: is the product really what is claimed?
- impact truth: does it really work?
- material truth: are composition and quality accurate?
- causal truth: does the product actually cause the claimed benefit?
- time truth: does it really last that long?
- transformation truth: does the company convert inputs into outputs efficiently?
- external impact truth: are environmental and social impacts correctly determined?
- potential truth: is growth potential real or merely a story?

A company with lower profits but high truth volume can be more valuable than a seemingly profitable company with weak reality.

The labor market changes fundamentally. Labor is paid not only for time but for generated truth volume.

New central activities emerge:

- observing phenomena
- verification
- falsification
- causal analysis
- comparison work
- impact testing
- matter determination
- transformation design
- randomness modeling
- decision architecture
- reality accounting
- education-volume creation

A wage can be:

\[
Wage = W_{time} + W_{knowledge} + W_{impact} + W_{causality} + W_{transformation}
\]

Repair work creates not only hours of labor, but fault determination, cause, solution, avoided downtime, and process improvement.

The person must not be valued as a person. Contributions, statements, work results, and effects are valued. Human dignity must never be converted into a market value.

## 9. Goods market

The goods market becomes a market of properties. One does not buy merely “a car,” but material state, safety, energy use, software behavior, durability, origin, failure probability, and recycling potential.

\[
Car_Q = Q_{matter} + Q_{function} + Q_{safety} + Q_{time} + Q_{impact} + Q_{potential}
\]

Products with low determination lose liquidity. Products with high determination become easier to trade because buyers carry less uncertainty.

## 10. Insurance market

Insurance trades randomness, time, causality, intensity, and impact. New forms emerge:

- causality insurance: protects against false cause-effect assumptions.
- falsification insurance: protects against the refutation of central assumptions.
- impact insurance: covers real impacts of a project.
- probability-space insurance: insures whole probability distributions.

A policy is:

\[
Q_{insurance} = (P(damage), intensity, cause, time, z, f)
\]

Insurance becomes more precise, but also more dependent on good models.

## 11. Asset and stock markets

Assets are reality bundles. A share is a claim on the future truth volume of a company:

\[
Share\ value = E\left(\sum_{t=1}^{n} z_t \cdot f_t \cdot h_t\right)
\]

Stock exchanges react not only to profit but to reality reports:

- product truth rises.
- potential truth falls.
- a causal assumption is falsified.
- impact volume becomes negative.
- education volume rises.
- transformation capacity improves.

Short selling partly becomes falsification trading. Whoever proves that a central company claim is false can move the market. This can reduce bubbles, but it can also produce manipulative falsification attacks.

---

## 12. Banks and credit

Banks finance not only assets but expected truth volume:

\[
Creditworthiness = E(z \cdot f \cdot h)
\]

Research, education, causal testing, material determination, and transformation can become creditworthy. Even a negative research result can be valuable if it ends a false direction.

## 13. Education and research

Education becomes direct wealth. It creates the ability to determine, compare, verify, falsify, and transform reality.

Research creates five main kinds of value:

1. positive verification
2. negative falsification
3. more precise intensity measurement
4. better causal structure
5. new transformation possibility

A result such as “this technology does not work” is not merely a failure. It is a tradable falsification value.

## 14. Commodity, energy, space, and logistics markets

Commodities are traded by mass, purity, origin, energy content, water impact, recyclability, and substitution potential.

Energy is traded by kilowatt-hours, timing, source, storability, grid stability, CO₂ impact, and conversion losses.

Real estate is valued by area, location, soil quality, climate risk, use potential, infrastructure access, and time development.

Logistics trades space, time, direction, condition, delay probability, and impact.

## 15. Global economic consequences

The world economy shifts from a money economy to a reality economy.

Today the question is often:

> Who has capital?

In this system the stronger question is:

> Who generates, owns, or controls truth-capable volume?

The strongest actors would be those with the best education, measurement systems, models, causal competence, fastest verification, most valuable falsification, and highest transformation capacity.

Countries with cheap production but poor determination lose advantages. Countries with high education, stable institutions, and good data quality gain.

## 16. Risks

The system is powerful and dangerous. The biggest risks are:

- truth monopolies
- measurement monopolies
- data colonialism
- surveillance
- reducing people to market values
- speculative truth bubbles
- falsification attacks
- private control over public reality

- open measurement standards
- decentralized verification
- independent science
- a public knowledge commons
- privacy protection
- the right to falsify
- antitrust law for data and measurement markets

## 17. Final formula

The planetary economy is:

\[
\mathcal{P} = (M, W, K, T, R, Z, A, E)
\]

Where:

- `M`: material markets
- `W`: knowledge markets
- `K`: causality markets
- `T`: time and transformation markets
- `R`: space and resource markets
- `Z`: randomness and insurance markets
- `A`: asset and stock markets
- `E`: effect and impact markets

Every object is a cuboid:

\[
Q_i = (S_i, z_i, f_i, h_i, d_i, t_i)
\]

The planetary total volume is:

\[
V_{planet} = \sum_i z_i \cdot f_i \cdot h_i
\]

But the sum alone is never enough. The sign matrix is always required:

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
+z,+f & +z,-f \\
-z,+f & -z,-f
\end{pmatrix}
\]

This matrix shows whether an economy consists mainly of verified values, valuable falsifications, real costs, or negated burdens.

The new wealth is:

> matter plus knowledge plus causality plus time plus impact plus truth.

The most important production factor is:

> the ability to determine, compare, verify, falsify, and meaningfully transform reality.

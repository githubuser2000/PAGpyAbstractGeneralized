# Topological Group Economy (TGE)

## A Complete Single-Currency Economic System for Countries, Groups, Planets, and Multiple Galaxies

**Currency:** Solution Number (**SN**)  
**Mathematical primitives:** Topology + algebraic group  
**Goal:** Problem-solving becomes economically positive. Problem-creation becomes economically negative.

---

## 0. Summary

The **Topological Group Economy**, abbreviated **TGE**, is an economic system with exactly one currency: the **Solution Number** (**SN**).

An SN is not a normal coin and not merely a database entry. An SN is the numerical value of a **stack** made from:

1. a topological context,
2. a group value,
3. an economic history,
4. an effect of problem-solving or problem-creation.

The core idea is:

```text
Topology + algebraic group → stack → number → currency
```

Positive SN arises when problems are solved, reduced, stabilized, or repaired.

Negative SN arises when problems are created, worsened, externalized, hidden, or fraudulently misrepresented.

The system is not magical. It does not replace physics, politics, courts, or ethics. But it makes one radical economic move:

```text
Whoever solves problems becomes positive.
Whoever creates problems becomes negative.
Whoever does both is accounted for honestly.
```

---

# 1. Mathematical Core

The system uses only two primitive structures:

1. an **algebraic group**,
2. a **topology**.

Everything else is built from these as an application, contract, institution, or protocol.

---

## 1.1 The Algebraic Group

The currency is based on the additive group of real numbers:

$$
G = (\mathbb{R}, +, 0, -)
$$

This means:

- There is addition.
- There is a neutral element \(0\).
- Every value has a negative inverse.
- Account balances can be positive or negative.

Examples:

$$
+1000 \text{ SN}
$$

means: the actor has net problem-solving value, credit, or claim.

$$
-1000 \text{ SN}
$$

means: the actor has net problem-creation, debt, open obligation, or repair burden.

The group provides **calculability**.

---

## 1.2 The Topology

There is a problem space:

$$
X
$$

On this problem space there is a topology:

$$
\tau
$$

An open set:

$$
U \in \tau
$$

represents a context in which problems, assets, or solutions belong together.

Examples of topological domains:

- water infrastructure for small cities,
- energy supply for cold planets,
- the labor market of a country,
- conflict resolution between states,
- galactic transport logistics,
- radiation shielding for outposts,
- credit default risk of an industry,
- food supply under resource scarcity.

The topology does not directly say how much something is worth. The group does that.

The topology says:

- Which problems are similar.
- Which solutions are transferable.
- Which assets are compatible.
- Which markets belong together.
- Which organizations, countries, planets, or galaxies are economically connectable.
- Where fusion is possible.
- Where adapter work, bridges, or translation are required.

The topology provides **compatibility**.

---

## 1.3 The Stack

A currency unit is a stack.

A stack consists of pairs:

$$
S = ((U_1,g_1),(U_2,g_2),...,(U_n,g_n))
$$

where:

$$
U_i \in \tau
$$

and:

$$
g_i \in G
$$

Each layer means:

```text
In this topological context, a specific positive or negative group value was created, transferred, or corrected.
```

The numerical value of the stack is:

$$
Z(S)=g_1+g_2+\dots+g_n
$$

The application domain of the stack is:

$$
D(S)=U_1 \cap U_2 \cap \dots \cap U_n
$$

If:

$$
D(S)\neq \varnothing
$$

then the stack is internally compatible.

If:

$$
D(S)=\varnothing
$$

then the stack does not fit together cleanly. It requires adapter work, clarification, standardization, or a new bridge.

Central principle:

```text
The group creates the value.
The topology decides whether things fit together.
```

# 2. Fusion, Combination, and Bundling

Two stacks can be combined:

$$
S \oplus T
$$

The value of the combination is:

$$
Z(S \oplus T)=Z(S)+Z(T)
$$

The topological application domain is:

$$
D(S \oplus T)=D(S)\cap D(T)
$$

This means:

- Values add.
- Contexts must overlap.
- If contexts are not compatible, a bridge is needed.

An engineer would say:

```text
Two modules can only be combined if their interfaces are compatible.
```

In the TGE, topology is the interface logic.

---

## 2.1 Example: Water Solution

A company has a water-purification solution for Planet A:

$$
S_A=(U_A,+500)
$$

Planet B has a similar water problem:

$$
P_B=(U_B,-800)
$$

If:

$$
U_A\cap U_B\neq \varnothing
$$

then the solution is probably transferable.

If:

$$
U_A\cap U_B=\varnothing
$$

then an adapter is needed:

$$
A=(U_{AB},+120)
$$

The adapter can itself be paid as engineering work.

# 3. The Currency: Solution Number (SN)

The only currency is the **Solution Number**, abbreviated **SN**.

An actor has an account:

$$
B(a)\in G
$$

Examples:

$$
B(a)=+10{,}000 \text{ SN}
$$

or:

$$
B(a)=-10{,}000 \text{ SN}
$$

---

## 3.1 Positive SN

Positive SN is created through:

- solved problems,
- reduced risks,
- repaired damage,
- created infrastructure,
- improved productivity,
- stabilized supply,
- successful mediation,
- verified technical improvement,
- knowledge that solves real problems,
- assets usable within a topological domain.

---

## 3.2 Negative SN

Negative SN is created through:

- damage,
- fraud,
- acts of war,
- environmental destruction,
- breach of contract,
- exploitation,
- defective systems,
- created instability,
- false claims of problem-solving,
- external costs shifted onto others.

Important:

```text
Negative SN evaluates actions, damages, obligations, and system states.
It does not evaluate the metaphysical worth of a person.
```

People are not “problem people.” Concrete effects are evaluated, not a person’s existence.

---

# 4. Basic Rule of Accounting

Every payment is group addition.

If A pays B:

$$
x \text{ SN}
$$

then:

$$
B(A):=B(A)-x
$$

$$
B(B):=B(B)+x
$$

Example:

A has:

$$
B(A)=100
$$

B has:

$$
B(B)=20
$$

A pays B 30 SN.

Afterward:

$$
B(A)=70
$$

$$
B(B)=50
$$

This is ordinary money arithmetic. The difference lies in where money comes from and why someone becomes negative.

---

# 5. How New SN Is Created

Positive SN is not created magically. Large positive SN is created only when there is a negative counter-stack, assignment, obligation, loan, or repair burden somewhere else.

Basic form:

$$
\text{Solver receives } +x
$$

$$
\text{Problem-holder, client, state, bank, or causer receives } -x
$$

Therefore:

```text
Positive SN are claims.
Negative SN are obligations.
```

---

$$
B(H)=+1000
$$

Craftsperson:

$$
B(W)=+100

$$
x=300

After repair:

$$
B(H)=700

$$

No new currency is created. This is a normal payment.

## 5.2 Public Problem

A country has a decaying bridge network.

State infrastructure account:

$$
B(S)=0
$$

Engineering consortium:

$$
B(I)=0
$$

The state opens a contract worth:

$$
x=1{,}000{,}000 \text{ SN}
$$

After performance:

$$
B(S)=-1{,}000{,}000
$$

$$
B(I)=+1{,}000{,}000
$$

The state has taken on an obligation. It is later balanced through taxes, fees, or other revenues.

---

## 5.3 Damage by a Causer

A corporation pollutes a body of water.

Damage:

$$
x=500{,}000 \text{ SN}
$$

Then:

$$
B(Corporation):=B(Corporation)-500{,}000
$$

A repair fund receives:

$$
B(RepairFund):=B(RepairFund)+500{,}000
$$

The repair fund then pays companies, workers, and scientists to fix the problem.

Damage is not merely morally judged. It is economically booked.

# 6. Problem Value

A problem state has a stack value.

Before:

$$
Z(S_0)
$$

After:

$$
Z(S_1)
$$

The change is:

$$
\Delta = Z(S_1)-Z(S_0)
$$

If:

$$
\Delta>0
$$

a problem was reduced or value was created.

If:

$$
\Delta<0
$$

a problem was created or worsened.

---

## 6.1 Example: Water Supply

A city has a water supply problem:

$$
Z(S_0)=-800
$$

After a project:

$$
Z(S_1)=-250
$$

Then:

$$
\Delta=-250-(-800)=+550
$$

The project has a solution value of:

$$
+550 \text{ SN}
$$

---

## 6.2 Example: Damage Creation

A company damages a previously clean area:

$$
Z(S_0)=0
$$

Afterward:

$$
Z(S_1)=-300
$$

Then:

$$
\Delta=-300-0=-300
$$

The company created a negative value of:

$$
-300 \text{ SN}
$$

The market price does not have to exactly equal this stack value. Prices still arise from supply, demand, risk, scarcity, and negotiation. The stack shows the technical and economic structure.

## 7.3 Labor Market

Labor power is the ability of a person or system to reduce problems in specific topological domains.

A worker does not sell “human worth.” A worker sells labor, time, know-how, responsibility, or results.

An employer pays SN.

Example monthly wage:

$$
+4{,}000 \text{ SN}
$$

For the employer:

$$
-4{,}000 \text{ SN}
$$

The employer accepts this because it expects the labor to create more value than it costs.

---

## 7.4 Problem-Solving Market

This is the most important market in the TGE.

Problems are posted there.

A problem contract has the form:

$$
P=((U_1,-g_1),(U_2,-g_2),...)
$$

A solver offers:

$$
L=((V_1,+h_1),(V_2,+h_2),...)
$$

Compatibility exists if:

$$
D(P)\cap D(L)\neq \varnothing
$$

If a solution does not fit directly, a market for adapter work emerges.

Adapter work is a central engineering service.

---

## 7.5 Capital Market

Capital is not a second currency.

- saved SN,
- assets with SN prices,
- shares in organizations,
- claims on future SN payments,
- machines,
- land,
- data,
- ships,
- infrastructure,

An investor gives SN to a company and receives ownership rights, profit claims, or repayment claims.

These claims are contracts. They are fulfilled in SN.

---

## 7.6 Risk Market and Insurance

Risk is a possible negative stack.

An insurance fund collects SN and pays out when damage occurs.

Example:

Many houses each pay:

$$
10 \text{ SN}
$$

into an insurance fund.

If one house burns down, the fund pays:

$$
5000 \text{ SN}
$$

for repair.

The fund evaluates risks topologically:

- similar regions,
- similar construction,
- similar dangers,
- similar maintenance conditions.

Here, topology roughly replaces risk classes.

- time,

- know-how,

An employment contract is a stack:

$$
EmploymentContract=((U_{Work},+Wage),(U_{Duties},-Liability),...)

## 8.3 Wage Types

All wages are denominated in SN.

Possible wage types:

1. **Time wage**  
   Payment for working time.

2. **Result wage**  
   Payment for completed tasks.

3. **Risk premium**  
   Payment for dangerous, difficult, or rare work.

4. **Topology premium**  
   Payment for work in hard-to-connect domains, meaning transfer between distant problem spaces.

5. **Repair-burden compensation**  
   Payment for work that reduces negative stacks created by other actors.

## 9.2 Profit

Profit is:

$$
Revenue-Expenses-Caused\ Negative\ Stacks
$$

If a company sells a lot but damages the environment, workers, or public stability, its true stack value is lower.

In an extreme case, a company may appear rich in ordinary bookkeeping while being systemically negative.

---

## 9.3 Merger of Companies

If companies A and B merge:

$$
S_{AB}=S_A\oplus S_B
$$

Value:

$$
Z(S_{AB})=Z(S_A)+Z(S_B)
$$

Application domain:

$$
D(S_{AB})=D(S_A)\cap D(S_B)
$$

If the domains do not fit, integration costs arise.

Example:

Company A can build energy systems.  
Company B can build medical systems.

If there is a shared domain, such as medical systems for remote planets, positive merger value emerges.

If there is no shared domain, the merger is merely a financial trick and may become negative.

---

## 9.4 Insolvency

If an organization becomes strongly negative:

$$
B(O)<0
$$

an insolvency procedure follows.

It includes:

- selling assets,
- reviewing contracts,
- identifying damages,
- separating fraud from failure,
- protecting employees,
- partially serving creditors,
- distributing negative stacks to responsible actors.

Rule:

```text
Merger or insolvency must not erase negative history.
```

Otherwise companies could create damage, dissolve themselves, and reappear.

## 10.2 Credit

A loan creates a positive and a negative stack.

Example:

A bank gives a company a 100,000 SN loan.

The company receives liquid funds:

$$
+100{,}000
$$

At the same time, a repayment obligation arises:

$$
-100{,}000
$$

Net, this is not magic. The company has received payment capacity, but also a negative obligation.

If the company uses the loan to solve a problem, it earns SN and repays.

If it fails, the negative obligation remains.

---

## 10.3 Banks Cannot Print Money Arbitrarily

A bank may lend only if it:

- has sufficient equity,
- discloses risks,
- can carry negative credit stacks,
- is controlled by auditors,
- receives negative SN if it certifies wrongly.

If a bank creates bad loans, that itself is a problem stack.

Then the bank becomes negative.

---

## 10.4 Central Bank or Currency Council

There is no central bank that can print SN arbitrarily.

But there is a **Clearing and Standards Council**.

This council does not decide the money supply arbitrarily. It manages:

- stack standards,
- verification rules,
- settlement protocols,
- dispute procedures,
- finality rules,
- bank supervision,
- interregional compatibility.

It is more like a measurement and clearing institution than a classical central bank.

It first falls on:

- the state,

- decision-makers,
- military or political organizations,

This prevents collective punishment.

---

## 11.5 War in the System

War almost always creates massive negative stacks.

Not only because of direct destruction, but because of:

- deaths,
- injuries,
- infrastructure loss,
- displacement,
- loss of trust,
- trade disruption,
- future risks,
- repair costs,
- political destabilization.

An attack therefore cannot be booked as profitable merely because loot was taken.

Loot may be a positive asset transfer, but the negative stacks caused by the attack are counted against it.

Aggressive war is usually massively negative.

Defense can be positive or neutral if it prevents greater damage. But defense also creates costs and damage that must be accounted for honestly.

Goal:

```text
Escalation becomes expensive.
Repair becomes valuable.
Stable cooperation becomes profitable.
```

# 14. Intergalactic Application

The intergalactic part is difficult because it must not be magical.

The main physical problem:

```text
No real-time settlement across galaxies.
```

The speed of light remains the speed of light.

Communication between galaxies may take extremely long.

Therefore the system needs local finality.

---

## 14.1 Causal Cells

The universe is divided into economic causal cells:

1. local cell, such as a city or station,
2. planetary cell,
3. stellar cell,
4. galactic cell,
5. intergalactic cell.

Each cell has local ledgers and banks.

A transaction inside a city can become final quickly.

A transaction between galaxies may only become final after a very long time.

---

## 14.2 One Currency, Delayed Finality

There remains only one currency:

```text
SN
```

But there are different finality levels:

- locally provisional,
- locally final,
- regionally confirmed,
- galactically confirmed,
- intergalactically confirmed.

This is not a second currency. It is only a question of how secure a payment already is.

---

## 14.3 Intergalactic Trade

Intergalactic trade does not usually involve ordinary daily goods. That would often be physically and economically unreasonable.

Useful intergalactic trade involves:

- knowledge,
- blueprints,
- solution patterns,
- ethically permissible medical data,
- long-lived research,
- maps,
- standards,
- cultural archives,
- highly compressed technology information,
- very long-term insurance,
- civilization rescue plans,
- astrophysical data,
- large asset rights.

A planet does not buy daily bread from another galaxy.

But one galaxy can buy a proven method for stabilizing star-system infrastructure.

---

## 14.4 Escrow Across Large Distances

In intergalactic deals, SN is locked in escrow.

A wants to buy a solution from B.

A places SN in escrow:

$$
B(A):=B(A)-x
$$

$$
B(Escrow):=B(Escrow)+x
$$

B delivers.

Local verifiers near B confirm delivery.

Later signals confirm or challenge the deal.

If everything is correct:

$$
B(Escrow):=B(Escrow)-x
$$

$$
B(B):=B(B)+x
$$

If fraud is found, inverse and negative stacks are booked.

## 17.1 Basic Rule Against Problem Farming

No one may profit by creating a problem and then selling its solution.

Rule:

$$
\text{self-caused damage} > \text{possible repair profit}
$$

If A causes 1000 SN of damage and later repairs it for 800 SN, A remains at:

$$
-200
$$

plus a penalty, liability, or trust-loss stack.

If A intentionally creates a problem, the negative assessment may exceed the pure repair value.

---

## 17.2 Correction Through Inverse Stacks

If a stack was wrong, it is not deleted. It is corrected through its inverse stack.

If someone wrongly received:

$$
+500
$$

then the system books:

$$
-500
$$

plus a fraud stack.

This is clean group accounting.

3. What is better afterward?
4. What side effects occurred?
5. Who benefited?
6. Who was harmed?
7. Who is responsible?
8. Is the solution transferable to similar topological domains?

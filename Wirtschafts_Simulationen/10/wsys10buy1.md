# Stacked Truth Values as Purchasing Power

Properly understood, this does **not** mean that one can buy truth itself. One can buy **transitions of truth**.

Not:

> “I buy truth.”

But rather:

> “I pay so that a certain statement about the world becomes truer, more probable, legally valid, socially recognized, or causally realizable.”

This is a powerful model. Money would only be the crude, one-dimensional surface. Beneath it lie stacked truth values: ownership, claim, delivery, ability, probability, trust, contract, perception, time-binding, and causality.

## 1. The Basic Formula

A purchase is:

> **Payment in exchange for a change in reality.**

Or more precisely:

> **I give up a bundled truth value so that another state of truth can be produced.**

Example: a car.

Before the purchase, the following statement is false or unsecured:

> “This car is available to me and can take me from A to B.”

After the purchase, registration, fueling, and confirmation that it works, the statement becomes truer:

> “I have a roadworthy car that can very probably drive.”

So you are not merely buying “a car.” You are buying a stack of truths:

- material existence: The car exists.
- ownership truth: It now belongs to me.
- technical truth: It works.
- legal truth: I am allowed to use it.
- causal truth: It can produce movement.
- temporal truth: It is available to me from now on.
- social truth: Others recognize my ownership.

The price compresses all of this into a number.

## 2. What Can Be Bought With This?

Almost everything that can be bought can be described as the **purchased making-true of a statement**.

### Living Space

You do not merely buy or rent square meters. You buy the truth:

> “I may stay in this place, others may not remove me from it without reason, and I can sleep, store things, and live there.”

This includes property rights, access, protection, duration, social recognition, utilities, location, neighborhood, and safety.

### Ticket / Arrival

A ticket does not buy the physical object called “ticket.” It buys the conditional truth:

> “I may use this transport system, and with some probability I will arrive at the destination.”

A plane ticket is a stack of seat claim, time window, transport obligation, infrastructure, route, risk, control, identity verification, and expectation.

### Product

A product is a compressed statement:

> “This thing can fulfill a certain function.”

A hammer means:

> “I can drive nails.”

A laptop means:

> “I can compute, write, communicate, and store information.”

A medicine means:

> “My bodily state will be changed with a certain probability.”

A beautiful chair means:

> “I can sit, but in addition, the room will be perceived differently.”

### Employee / Service

Here one must be precise: You do not buy the person. You buy contractually limited time, attention, ability, and responsibility.

So you buy the truth:

> “For this duration, this person directs part of their agency toward my goal.”

That is purchased causality through another person’s competence.

### Beauty / “Being More Beautiful”

One does not buy beauty as an absolute truth. One buys conditions that make a statement more likely:

> “I will be perceived by myself or by others as more beautiful.”

This includes clothing, training, skincare, surgery, lighting, status, style, context, comparison group, and cultural codes.

Beauty is therefore a hybrid truth value: partly biological, partly social, partly aesthetic, partly self-perceptual.

### Adventure Vacation / Intensity

An adventure vacation does not simply buy travel time. It buys an increased density of certain events:

> “Within a short time, many intense, new, physical, risky, or memorable states will occur.”

That is purchased intensity. One pays for probability, staging, change of place, adrenaline, social narratability, and later memory.

## 3. Causality as a Commodity

The strongest idea is probably this:

> **One buys causalities.**

A purchase is often the acquisition of a mechanism:

> “If I have X, I can cause Y.”

Examples:

- A drill buys the causality “hole in wall.”
- A car buys the causality “movement without a timetable.”
- A lawyer buys the causality “improvement of legal position.”
- A designer buys the causality “change in how others perceive something.”
- A teacher buys the causality “increase in future ability.”
- Insurance buys the causality “damage is financially buffered.”

So: One does not only buy things. One buys **capacities to produce effects**.

## 4. Buying Time

Time can also be bought, but not as pure time. Usually one buys one of four forms:

1. **Other people’s time**  
   Employees, service providers, drivers, assistants.

2. **Saved time**  
   Taxi instead of walking, premium service instead of waiting, software instead of manual labor.

3. **Reserved time**  
   Appointment, rental duration, hotel night, time slot.

4. **Future openness**  
   Education, capital, options, health, network.

“Buying time” usually means:

> “I pay so that a desired state becomes true earlier, longer, more safely, or with less effort of my own.”

## 5. Determination and Comparison

Comparisons are also truth values.

Examples:

> “A is faster than B.”  
> “This apartment is larger than that one.”  
> “This vacation is more intense.”  
> “This person appears more competent.”  
> “This product is more durable.”

A market constantly trades in comparative truths. Prices arise not only from things, but from relations:

- better than
- rarer than
- faster than
- more beautiful than
- closer than
- safer than
- more prestigious than
- more likely to succeed than

A luxury product often sells less function than comparative truth:

> “I own something that others do not own.”

## 6. Buying Potentials

Potentials are especially interesting because they are not fully realized truths. They are **not yet true, but capable of becoming true**.

Examples:

- tool
- education
- plot of land
- network
- talent contract
- stock
- medicine
- gym membership

In such cases, one does not buy the finished state, but the statement:

> “Under suitable conditions, this desired state can emerge.”

Potential is therefore a stacked truth value directed toward the future.

## 7. Debt as a Negative Truth Value

The idea of “minus” is very strong.

Debt is not merely a negative number. Debt is a **negative claim on reality**.

If I have 100 euros of debt, the following statement is true:

> “A part of my future agency already belongs to someone else.”

Or:

> “I must perform a truth-balancing act in the future.”

Debt arises when a truth is brought forward prematurely.

Example: credit.

Now the following becomes true:

> “I can buy today.”

But at the same time, this also becomes true:

> “I owe payment tomorrow.”

That is a negative stack. It burdens the future, freedom, credibility, and room for action.

A lie is also a kind of epistemic debt:

> “I assert a truth that is not backed by reality.”

Then a deficit emerges between statement and reality. Sooner or later it must be settled: through correction, concealment, loss of trust, punishment, or alteration of reality.

## 8. Truth Has Several Layers

For this model to work, “truth” must not be merely binary. There are at least five different layers.

### Ontic Truth

What is actually the case?

Example:

> The car exists and has an engine.

### Epistemic Truth

What is known, checked, or proven?

Example:

> The inspection report says it is roadworthy.

### Social Truth

What do others recognize?

Example:

> The registration authority recognizes you as the holder.

### Legal Truth

What is institutionally valid?

Example:

> You are allowed to drive, insure, and sell it.

### Causal Truth

What can be brought about through it?

Example:

> The car can transport you.

A “stacked truth value” would therefore not simply be `true` or `false`, but more like a vector:

> Existence + proof + law + recognition + function + probability + time-binding + risk

Money brutally turns this into a number. This model tries to make the hidden dimensions visible again.

## 9. The Core Sentence

The core could be:

> **To buy means to exchange truth values for causal rights.**

Or even stronger:

> **A price is the numerical form of a stacked truth-claim on reality.**

This is philosophically and economically fruitful.

Money is then not “value in itself,” but a claim on future truth-making:

> “With this amount, I can cause others to make certain statements about my future true.”

For example:

> “I will have shelter.”  
> “I will eat.”  
> “I will arrive.”  
> “I will appear more beautiful.”  
> “I will be protected.”  
> “I will save time.”  
> “I will have access.”  
> “I will make someone act.”  
> “I will reduce a risk.”  
> “I will possess a possibility.”

## 10. What Cannot Be Bought

The boundary matters.

One cannot directly buy:

> “The past was different.”

One can forge documents, influence memory, buy narratives, or suppress evidence. But that does not buy truth. It buys control over perception.

One also cannot logically buy:

> “2 + 2 = 5.”

One can only corrupt social systems so that they claim it. Then social pseudo-truth increases, but ontic or logical truth remains unchanged.

One cannot buy absolute love. But one can buy conditions that make love, attachment, desire, or dependency more likely.

One cannot securely buy health. But one can buy probabilities, diagnosis, treatment, prevention, and care.

The model must therefore distinguish between **truth**, **probability**, **recognition**, and **manipulation**.

## 11. The Danger: False Truth Stacks

A market can become sick when the stacked truth values are false.

Examples:

- A product promises more function than it has.
- A person sells competence they do not possess.
- An institution issues certificates without proper examination.
- A bank creates payment claims without real backing.
- A brand sells status that only works through illusion.
- A state declares something valid even though reality works against it.

Then truth bubbles emerge.

A bubble is:

> **Too much socially recognized truth with too little real causality.**

When it bursts, the truth stack is revalued. Debt, fraud, disappointment, and falling prices are then forms of reality returning.

## 12. The Most Precise Answer

What can one buy with stacked truth values?

One can buy:

> **States, rights, access, probabilities, causalities, time-bindings, intensities, comparisons, potentials, recognitions, and obligations.**

But always in this form:

> **A statement about reality is supposed to become true, remain true, become more probable, or be treated as true by others.**

The purchase is therefore a transaction between statement and world.

A very compressed term for this would be:

> **truth capital.**

And debts would be:

> **negative truth obligations toward the future.**

This gives us a powerful system:

> **Economics as the trade of stacked, temporally directed truth values.**

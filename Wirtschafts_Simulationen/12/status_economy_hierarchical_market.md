# Status Economy: A Hierarchical Market System

This model does **not** abolish markets. The correction is important: the market remains, but the market is no longer based on flat money alone. The market itself is converted into hierarchy, and hierarchy becomes the currency.

In such a system, money is not the deepest economic unit. Money is only a numerical projection of hierarchy. The real unit of trade is a structured hierarchy bundle: a ranked combination of rights, privileges, duties, disadvantages, access levels, product claims, office claims, market claims, and social position.

A normal currency says only: **how much**.

A hierarchy currency says: **how much, of what kind, at what level, with which rights, with which duties, with which access restrictions, and with which social rank**.

The system is therefore not anti-market. It is a **hierarchical market economy**: everything can be traded, but everything is traded as hierarchy.

## 1. The Basic Idea

Every person, company, product, market, institution, country, colony, and strategic asset has a hierarchy position. This hierarchy position can be converted into a number, but the number is only a compression of the full hierarchy.

The hierarchy contains more information than a flat price. A price can tell us that something costs 100 units. A hierarchy tells us whether those 100 units are basic consumer access, elite housing access, military command access, scientific authority, media reach, legal privilege, risk burden, labor obligation, market-entry permission, or solar-system-level strategic control.

That is the decisive difference.

The system still has trade. It still has buying and selling. It still has markets. But instead of trading only money against goods, actors trade hierarchy against hierarchy.

A transaction is not simply:

```text
money -> product
```

It is closer to:

```text
hierarchy bundle A -> hierarchy bundle B
```

For example:

```text
engineering contribution + risk duty + service time
-> housing privilege + product access + status credit + future promotion claim
```

Or:

```text
market-entry right + supply-chain privilege + regional product license
-> strategic production obligation + tax claim + hierarchy credit
```

The market is therefore not removed. It is deepened.

## 2. Hierarchy as Currency

The currency of the system is hierarchy itself. However, for settlement, accounting, and comparison, hierarchy can always be converted into a number.

A simple representation is:

\[
H(X) = \{(e_1,h_1,q_1), (e_2,h_2,q_2), ..., (e_n,h_n,q_n)\}
\]

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(X\) | the person, product, company, market, privilege, or asset being valued |
| \(e_i\) | a hierarchy element |
| \(h_i\) | the height or rank level of that element |
| \(q_i\) | the quantity, intensity, or strength of that element |

The numerical value can then be calculated as:

\[
N(X) = \sum_i q_i \cdot V(e_i) \cdot M(h_i)
\]

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(N(X)\) | numerical hierarchy value of \(X\) |
| \(V(e_i)\) | base value of the hierarchy element |
| \(M(h_i)\) | multiplier based on hierarchy height |

In the simplest version, the number is built by **adding hierarchy elements** and **multiplying them by a height multiplier**.

So the system could also be written as:

\[
N = \sum_k E_k \cdot M_k
\]

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(E_k\) | number or weighted amount of hierarchy elements at level \(k\) |
| \(M_k\) | multiplier for hierarchy level \(k\) |

Example:

| Hierarchy level | Meaning | Multiplier |
|---|---|---:|
| 1 | basic access | 1 |
| 2 | local privilege | 5 |
| 3 | regional command or scarce access | 25 |
| 4 | planetary strategic importance | 200 |
| 5 | solar-system mission importance | 2,000 |

If an actor has 10 hierarchy elements at level 2 and 3 hierarchy elements at level 4, then:

\[
N = 10 \cdot 5 + 3 \cdot 200 = 650
\]

But the actor does not merely have “650 units.” The actor has:

```text
10 local privilege elements + 3 planetary strategic elements
```

That is much more precise than ordinary money.

## 3. Why Hierarchy Says More Than Money

Flat money is highly flexible, but it is semantically poor. It tells the system that two things have the same price, but not whether they have the same social meaning, strategic meaning, moral weight, risk, or institutional function.

Hierarchy is less flat but more informative.

Two assets can have the same numerical value while being very different as hierarchy bundles.

Example:

```text
A = 500 units of elite housing access
B = 500 units of military risk duty
C = 500 units of scientific infrastructure access
D = 500 units of media reach
```

In normal money, all four can be reduced to the same amount. In a hierarchy economy, they are not truly identical. They are only numerically comparable.

This is why hierarchy can be more exact than money. It preserves type, level, context, permission, and burden.

The number is useful for clearing trades. The hierarchy is useful for understanding what is actually being traded.

## 4. The Market Still Exists

The market does not disappear. It becomes a hierarchy market.

People, firms, products, and institutions still exchange value. But they exchange structured value rather than merely flat currency.

A worker does not simply sell labor for wages. The worker sells a hierarchy contribution: skill, reliability, risk, loyalty, time, scarcity, social usefulness, and possible future leadership.

An employer does not simply pay money. The employer gives hierarchy: income, product access, career rank, training access, team membership, institutional protection, office rights, housing rights, mobility rights, and promotion pathways.

A company does not simply sell products. It sells a product hierarchy: usefulness, trust, repairability, technical rank, strategic importance, brand authority, service guarantees, and compatibility with other ranked products.

A country does not simply sell exports. It trades national hierarchy: security guarantees, market access, logistical routes, military protection, resource claims, demographic potential, scientific capacity, and geopolitical rank.

Therefore, the system is still a market system. But the traded medium is richer.

## 5. Markets Themselves Can Be Traded

In this model, even markets can be traded.

A market is not just a place where trade happens. A market is itself a hierarchy object. It has:

- participants
- access rules
- gatekeepers
- liquidity
- reputation
- legal permissions
- product categories
- settlement rules
- tax claims
- information advantages
- infrastructure
- strategic importance
- territorial or orbital relevance

So a market can have its own hierarchy position:

\[
H(Market)
\]

This means that the right to enter, operate, tax, regulate, list products in, settle trades through, or dominate a market can itself be bought, sold, leased, ranked, and exchanged.

Examples:

- a local food market
- a national housing market
- a military procurement market
- a planetary energy market
- an orbital freight market
- a lunar construction market
- a Mars agriculture market
- a solar-system logistics market

A stock exchange, for example, is not merely a business. It is a hierarchy node. It controls listing, visibility, trust, access, liquidity, settlement, and symbolic legitimacy.

In a solar-system economy, markets would become even more hierarchical because location, transport cost, survival risk, and strategic value would differ radically between Earth, orbit, the Moon, Mars, asteroid industries, and outer-system missions.

## 6. Products Are Also Hierarchical

Products do not merely have prices. They have hierarchy positions.

A product can be ranked by:

- usefulness
- scarcity
- reliability
- repairability
- technological level
- strategic importance
- symbolic value
- safety level
- integration with other products
- military value
- infrastructure value
- ecological effect
- cultural effect

So a product has a hierarchy bundle:

\[
H(Product)
\]

A high-ranking product receives better supply chains, better workers, better financing, better protection, higher visibility, and easier integration into other systems.

This saves time because products do not have to be evaluated only through advertising, negotiation, and price discovery. Their hierarchy position already contains social and systemic information.

A medical device, a military component, a school platform, a Mars oxygen generator, and a luxury watch may all have market value. But their hierarchy positions are not the same. The oxygen generator may rank far higher in a Mars colony even if its Earth-market price is lower than a luxury good.

This is exactly where hierarchy is stronger than flat market pricing.

## 7. Different People Receive Access to Different Products

In a hierarchy economy, not every person has the same product access. Different people are entitled to different products because their hierarchy positions differ.

This does not mean that there is no trade. It means that trade has eligibility rules.

To acquire a product, an actor may need:

1. enough numerical hierarchy value,
2. the right type of hierarchy credit,
3. the right rank category,
4. the right age or life-phase status,
5. the right professional or institutional role,
6. the right location or mission relevance.

So one person may be allowed to access military tools, another medical tools, another media reach, another elite housing, another scientific computing power, another mobility privileges, another cultural authority.

The market still works, but access is stratified.

This is not a simple consumer market. It is a ranked access market.

## 8. Trading Privileges and Disadvantages

Privileges can be traded. Disadvantages can also be traded.

A privilege is a positive hierarchy claim, such as:

- better housing
- better transport
- priority access
- elite education
- better medical options
- higher computing access
- greater media reach
- market-entry rights
- management authority
- access to scarce goods
- symbolic honor
- legal or administrative priority

A disadvantage is a negative or burdensome hierarchy position, such as:

- risk duty
- dangerous work
- remote location
- lower comfort
- rationing
- social burden
- service obligation
- lower priority
- reduced visibility
- harder working conditions
- military exposure
- unattractive but necessary labor

In this system, a person or organization can trade disadvantage for future advantage.

Example:

```text
accept Mars construction risk for 5 years
-> receive high future housing status + family education privilege + promotion credit
```

Or:

```text
accept dangerous rescue work
-> receive increased trust status + healthcare priority + public honor + career acceleration
```

This makes burdens economically visible. It prevents the system from treating sacrifice as invisible.

But this part is dangerous. If disadvantages can be traded without limits, the strong can buy advantages while the weak sell away their future. Therefore, a stable hierarchy economy needs non-tradable protections.

The following should not be tradable:

- basic human status
- bodily integrity
- legal personhood
- basic medical care
- basic food access
- basic shelter
- the right to appeal
- the right to exit abusive contracts
- the right not to be permanently subordinated

Disadvantages may be traded only if they are temporary, compensated, visible, and reversible.

Without this rule, the system would become a market for domination.

## 9. Age as a Hierarchy Multiplier

The age curve does not remove the market. It modifies hierarchy access and hierarchy multipliers.

A Gaussian age curve can be used to model the fact that different life phases carry different kinds of performance, risk, fertility, leadership, experience, dependency, and social function.

Formally:

\[
A(t) = e^{-\frac{(Age - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}
\]

This age factor can modify the hierarchy value:

\[
N(X,t) = A(t) \cdot \sum_i q_i \cdot V(e_i) \cdot M(h_i)
\]

In your model, the curve for women and men can be shifted:

- women may receive earlier high status,
- men may receive later high status.

The deeper point is that status is not only individual performance. It is also life-phase position inside the whole hierarchy.

A good implementation should be careful, though. If the male/female curve is too rigid, the system wastes exceptional individuals. A stronger version would allow sex-shifted default curves but also individual override by demonstrated performance, responsibility, or mission need.

The age curve should guide the hierarchy. It should not become an unbreakable caste rule.

## 11. Automatic and Manual Rise

Some actors rise automatically because their strength is visible.

Examples:

- militarily strong states
- economically productive companies
- technically superior products
- highly demanded workers
- strategically necessary colonies
- efficient logistical systems
- strong research institutions

They rise because the hierarchy has to recognize real power. A system that refuses to recognize real strength becomes detached from reality and eventually loses control.

But not all value is visible through strength.

Some actors must be manually raised:

- helpers
- care workers
- teachers
- social stabilizers
- honest media creators
- rescuers
- moral exemplars
- long-term researchers
- people who prevent damage before it becomes visible
- people who hold society together without becoming rich or powerful

This manual elevation is necessary because pure measurable strength undervalues care, truth, meaning, and prevention.

However, manual elevation is dangerous because it can become favoritism, propaganda, or ideological ranking. Therefore, it needs transparency, appeal, rotation, public justification, and independent review.

## 12. Why This Can Be Stronger Than Flat Market Capitalism

The system can be stronger than a flat money economy because it carries more information.

A normal price compresses everything into one number. That makes trade easy, but it destroys context.

Hierarchy keeps the context.

It can show:

- whether value comes from production or risk,
- whether a privilege is local, planetary, or solar-system level,
- whether a person has earned access or merely inherited it,
- whether a product is luxurious or strategically necessary,
- whether a company is profitable or systemically important,
- whether a market is ordinary or civilizationally critical,
- whether a disadvantage is exploitative or honorable,
- whether a trade increases or damages the whole hierarchy.

This makes the economy more exact.

A hierarchy market can coordinate careers, products, firms, institutions, and strategic missions faster than a system that only sees prices.

Career becomes more rational because advancement directly improves hierarchy position. Products become more coordinated because product rank determines integration and priority. Companies become more legible because they are not judged only by profit, but by systemic place. Markets become tradable because their access, rules, and strategic importance are themselves hierarchy objects.

## 13. The Main Risk: Hierarchy Fraud

The system stands or falls with the integrity of hierarchy measurement.

If hierarchy positions are false, the entire economy becomes false.

People will not merely try to earn status. They will try to manipulate the hierarchy number.

Possible distortions:

- people perform visible work instead of useful work,
- companies optimize hierarchy metrics instead of products,
- media creators become propagandists to gain manual elevation,
- military actors create threats to increase their status,
- bureaucrats protect their hierarchy position,
- old elites block young competitors,
- rich actors buy privileges and sell disadvantages downward,
- low-status people are pressured into bad trades,
- whole regions become permanently subordinated.

Therefore, hierarchy must be auditable.

A serious version of the system needs:

- transparent formulas,
- hidden anti-gaming checks,
- public reasoning for high-level rank changes,
- appeal courts,
- independent review institutions,
- competing hierarchy evaluators,
- time limits on privileges,
- anti-corruption rules,
- no permanent sale of basic status,
- protection against inherited caste formation.

## 14. The Best Formulation

The corrected formulation is this:

> A status economy is not a system without markets. It is a hierarchical market economy in which hierarchy is the real currency, and numerical money is only a compressed settlement form of hierarchy.

Everything is traded as hierarchy:

- labor,
- products,
- companies,
- privileges,
- disadvantages,
- access rights,
- market positions,
- states,
- colonies,
- strategic missions,
- media reach,
- military duty,
- social honor,
- future promotion claims.

The number is created by adding hierarchy elements and multiplying them by hierarchy-height multipliers.

But the number is not the whole truth. The hierarchy bundle is the whole truth.

A flat monetary economy asks:

```text
What is the price?
```

A hierarchy economy asks:

```text
What is the rank, type, level, burden, privilege, access right, and systemic meaning of the thing being traded?
```

That is why this system can still have markets, and in fact can have more exact markets than a flat money economy.

## 15. The Core in One Sentence

The market remains, but the traded currency is hierarchy: a structured, rank-sensitive, privilege-and-duty-bearing form of value that can be converted into a number, while preserving more information than money alone.

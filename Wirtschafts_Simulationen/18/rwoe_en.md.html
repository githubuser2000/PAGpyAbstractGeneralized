Relational Monetary Economy: a complete economic system based on a currency that is not a number

1. Basic idea: a currency that is not a number

I call the system here:

Relational Monetary Economy

or, briefly:

RME

The currency of this economy is not a number such as “100 euros”, but a tradable relational table.

Not:

I have 100.

But:

I have a bundle of rights, duties, claims, risks, counterparties, time bindings, resource links, abilities, securities and social costs.

Money is therefore not understood as a one-dimensional amount, but as a structured market state.

The basic unit is not the coin, not the banknote, not the account balance, but a relation table:

Relation ID Subject Object Relationship type Claim Duty Period Condition Transferability Risk Legal status Proof
R-001 Person A Firm B Work 20 hours skilled labor Payment/protection June Contract active limited medium valid signed
R-002 Firm B Machine C Use 100 hours machine time Maintenance Q3 workshop only yes low valid verified
R-003 State Citizen A Tax claim Contribution Infrastructure yearly income/consumption no low statutory registry
R-004 Person D Person A Debt Repayment Payment duty 6 months due yes high disputed contract

This table is not merely documentation. It is itself the carrier of value.

A payment would then not be:

A pays B 500.

But:

A transfers to B a bundle of valid relations:
- claim to delivery
- claim to labor performance
- claim against a third party
- right to use a resource
- tax credit
- insurance coverage
- reputation guarantee

That is the core.

2. What is different from a normal market economy?

The normal market economy condenses almost everything into a price.

Good → price
Labor → wage
Risk → interest
Damage → cost
Resource → market value

That is powerful because it is simple.

But it is also blind. A number does not show why something is valuable, to whom something is owed, which risks are attached, which social costs arise, which dependencies are created, or which power relations are sold along with it.

The relational monetary economy says:

The price is too poor.
The market needs more than numbers.
The market needs structured relationships.

That is why, in a specific sense, this system is stronger than a market economy:

A market economy trades prices.
A relational monetary economy trades structured reality.

But this is exactly where the danger lies.

If everything is captured as a relation and made tradable, then not only goods are traded more efficiently. Dependencies, biographies, labor power, social ties, debt, access, control and human vulnerability also become tradable.

That creates an enormous economic boom — and an enormous moral threat.

3. The central thesis

The RME operates according to this thesis:

Everything economic is a relation.

Not only things have value, but relationships:

A person owns a thing.
A person owes a performance.
A firm needs labor power.
The state levies tax.
A customer trusts a provider.
A machine creates a product.
Land carries yield.
Data creates forecasts.
Damage creates liability.
Risk creates demand for insurance.
Scarcity creates bargaining power.

In the classical market economy, these relations are often hidden and finally pressed into a price.

In the RME they remain visible and become currency themselves.

4. The currency: the Relational Value Bundle

The actual currency is called here:

Relational Value Bundle

Briefly:

RVB

An RVB is a table of tradable relations.

It contains not only “value”, but the structure of value.

An RVB can contain:

Category Example
Ownership relations A owns machine X
Use rights B may use warehouse Y for 30 days
Labor claims C owes 10 hours of work
Delivery claims D delivers 500 units of raw material
Debt claims E owes F repayment
Insurance relations G covers risk H
Tax relations The state accepts the claim as a contribution
Reputation relations An auditor confirms quality
Liability relations The causer bears the damage
Data relations A data set authorizes analysis/use
Environmental relations An activity creates emission or compensation
Social relations Dependency, duty of care, duty of protection

Important:

The currency is not a number.
The currency is a bundle of relationships.

Numbers can stand in cells, such as hours, quantities, deadlines or risk values. But the currency itself is not that number. It is the entire table.

5. The basic structure of every currency table

Every currency table needs at least these columns:

Column Meaning
Subject Who has a claim or a duty?
Counterparty Against whom is the relation directed?
Object What does the relation refer to?
Relationship type Ownership, labor, debt, use, liability, delivery, etc.
Claim What may be demanded?
Duty What must be performed?
Period When does it apply?
Condition Under which circumstances does it apply?
Transferability May the relation be resold?
Divisibility May it be split?
Enforceability Can it be legally enforced?
Risk How likely are default, fraud or dispute?
Proof Who confirms the relation?
Rank Does it have priority over other claims?
Moral status permitted, restricted, prohibited, inalienable
Publicity private, semi-public, state, anonymized
Deletability ends through fulfillment, expiry, revocation, death, etc.

Every “unit of money” thereby becomes a mini-contract, mini-asset register and mini-risk profile.

6. Payment in this system

In the normal economy:

A buys goods from B.
A pays 100 euros.
B hands over the goods.

In the relational monetary economy:

A buys goods from B.
A transfers to B a suitable bundle of relations.
B accepts it because it fits B's need.

Example:

B sells a bicycle. B does not demand “300 euros”, but:

B accepts:
- a valid claim to 5 hours of repair work
- plus a state-accepted tax relation
- plus a transferable claim against C
- plus a guarantee against default

A therefore pays not with a number, but with a structured table:

Relation Content Why B accepts it
Labor claim A repairs machines for 5 hours B needs repair
Tax relation creditable public claim B can lower tax duty
Claim C owes delivery B can resell it
Guarantee default is covered risk is reduced

The price is no longer a number. The price is an acceptance pattern.

A price is a table that the seller accepts.
A payment is a table that the buyer transfers.
A market is a system that brings matching tables together.

7. How do values arise?

Value does not arise simply by counting rows.

A table with 1,000 worthless, risky or prohibited relations can be worth less than a table with 3 very strong relations.

Value arises from:

usefulness
scarcity
enforceability
trust
transferability
time proximity
risk content
social acceptance
state recognition
productivity
legal clarity

A relation bundle is strong when many market participants accept it.

A relation bundle is weak when it is hard to enforce, morally burdened, risky, illiquid or legally prohibited.

8. The role of numbers

The RME does not forbid numbers. It only demotes them.

Numbers are no longer the currency itself. Numbers are only auxiliary values inside the tables.

Examples:

10 hours
3 months
50 kilowatt-hours
2 hectares
5% default risk
40 tons of CO₂
8 deliveries

But the value does not lie in the single number, but in the relation:

10 hours from whom?
For whom?
What work?
Under which conditions?
With what quality?
With what risk?
With what legal protection?
Transferable or not?
Voluntary or coerced?

That is the difference.

The market economy asks:

What does it cost?

The RME asks:

Which relationship is transferred?
Which power is transferred?
Which duty is transferred?
Which future is transferred?
Which dependency is created?

That makes it stronger — but also more dangerous.

A house then consists not only of house + owner, but of:

In a classical market economy, the house might appear as:

In the RME it appears as:

A bundle of rights, duties, risks, yields, costs, bindings and access possibilities.

That is more realistic.

A house is not just a thing. It is a node in a network of rights and duties.

An enterprise consists of:

labor relations

ownership relations

permit relations

tax relations

An enterprise is rich when its relations are:

productive
recognized
liquid
legally stable
scarce

An enterprise is weak when its relations are:

disputed

dependent
short-term

morally toxic
not transferable

Profit in the RME does not simply mean:

revenues minus costs.

Profit means:

After production, the enterprise creates a better relation bundle than before.

That means:

more accepted claims
fewer toxic duties
better delivery relationships
better reputation
higher enforceability
less risk
more future options

This is broader than monetary profit.

11. Labor in the RME

Here lies the decisive moral conflict.

In a legitimate version of the RME, the human being is not traded, but only a voluntarily limited labor relation.

Permissible would be:

Person A voluntarily undertakes,
for firm B,
in period T,
under conditions C,
to provide a specific performance L,
with right of termination,
protective rights,
wage claim,
working-time limit,
non-transferability of the person.

That is a labor relation.

It can be represented as a table:

Person Employer Performance Period Duty Counter-performance Termination Protective rights Transferability
A Firm B Skilled labor 3 months Work payment/insurance yes yes restricted

The critical boundary is:

The labor performance can be the object of a contract.
The human being himself or herself must not be.

Without this boundary, the system tips over.

12. Capital in the RME

Capital is no longer only money or property, but any relation that creates future power to act.

Capital can be:

machine use right
access to data
customer trust
license
patent
work commitment
supply chain
state permit
cheap credit
social reputation
insurance cover
access to raw materials

The RME thereby makes visible what the market economy often conceals:

Capital is condensed relationship power.

Whoever owns many strong relations owns power.

Not only whoever has much money.

13. Credit in the RME

Credit is a future relation.

In the normal economy:

A bank grants a loan of 100,000 euros.
The debtor repays later.

In the RME:

The bank creates a recognized relation bundle
that contains future performances, securities, risks and legal claims.

A credit RVB contains:

Element Meaning
Debtor Who owes?
Creditor To whom is it owed?
Future performance What is expected?
Securities Which relations are liable?
Rank Who is served first?
Risk Probability of default
Purpose Production, consumption, speculation
Control Which intervention rights does the creditor receive?
Default What happens in non-fulfillment?

Banks become relation transformers in this system.

They take relations that are hard to trade and turn them into relations that are easier to accept.

Example:

Many uncertain repayment promises
→ bundled, checked, insured
→ tradable credit bundle

That is powerful.

But it can also become toxic, just like securitizations in financial crises — only much deeper, because not only debts but all life relationships can be packaged.

14. Central bank and money supply

A normal central bank manages money supply, interest and liquidity.

The central bank of the RME manages:

recognition
standardization
trust ranks
public acceptance
minimum quality of relations
classes of securities
deletion rules
transferability rules
risk weights
crisis cleanup

It does not only ask:

How much money is in circulation?

But:

Which relations are in circulation?
How good are they?
How toxic are they?
How strongly are they nested?
Who depends on whom?
Which relations may count as general means of payment?
Which relations threaten freedom, stability or dignity?

Inflation in this system is not merely price increase.

Inflation means:

Too many weak, insecure or overstretched relations are accepted as valuable.

Deflation means:

Too few relations are accepted.
Everyone holds only hard, safe, state-guaranteed relations.
Exchange stalls.

A crisis arises when many market participants suddenly recognize:

The relations that we accepted as currency cannot be fulfilled.

Then not only trust in money collapses, but trust in relationships.

15. The state

In the RME, the state is not only regulator, but the most important moral and legal boundary setter.

It does five things:

1. It defines inalienable relations

For example:

human dignity
bodily freedom
basic rights
freedom to terminate employment
protection against coercion
protection against debt bondage
data protection
basic family rights
political rights

These must not be traded.

2. It makes certain relations acceptable for tax purposes

The state can say:

I accept these relations for the fulfillment of public duties.

Example:

care work
CO₂ compensation
infrastructure contribution
money equivalent
public service
tax claim

This creates demand for certain relations.

3. It prohibits toxic relations

Such as:

trade in persons
unlimited labor binding
coercive contracts
non-terminable debt relations
sold voting rights
sold bodily rights
unlimited data control
forced dependency relations

4. It protects weaker parties

Because a relational market is far more powerful than a money market, stronger protective rights are needed.

labor law
tenancy law
consumer protection
data protection
debtor protection
insolvency law
antitrust law
anti-discrimination law
minimum standards

5. It stabilizes crises

When relation markets freeze, the state must provide safe public relations:

basic supply
public work
basic claims
state-guaranteed payment relations
debt cuts
relation deletion
conversion of toxic relations

Without the state, the RME quickly becomes a private economy of domination.

16. Markets in the RME

A market is no longer merely a place where prices are formed.

A market is a place where relations are made compatible.

There are different markets:

Market What is traded
Goods market ownership and delivery relations
Labor market limited labor-performance relations
Capital market future claims, securities, participations
Credit market debt and repayment relations
Insurance market risk assumption
Data market use rights to data
Energy market production and consumption relations
Housing market use, rental and ownership relations
Reputation market certifications, audits, trust signals
Environmental market emissions, compensation, regeneration
Care market care and support relations
State market tax, license and permit relations

The market mechanism is:

Offer table meets demand table.
A clearing system checks compatibility.
If the relations fit, exchange takes place.

The price is therefore not the central information. The central information is:

Do these relations fit together?

17. Competition

Competition does not consist only in being cheaper.

Competition consists in offering better relations.

Better means:

more reliable
faster
lower risk
more legally secure
eco-friendlier
more socially accepted
easier to transfer
better combinable
more transparent
less exploitative

An enterprise wins not only because it has the lowest price, but because its relations are accepted in more markets.

This is one of the reasons why the system is stronger:

The classical market economy often optimizes for price.
The RME optimizes for the connectivity of relationships.

18. Why this system is stronger than a market economy

“Stronger” does not automatically mean “better” or “fairer” here.

Stronger means:

more information
more access
more steerability
more tradability
more mobilization of hidden values
more control over dependencies
more ability to pledge the future
more ability to compare complex goods

The RME is stronger for several reasons.

First: it makes invisible values visible

In the market economy, many things are poorly represented:

trust
care
social stability
ecological damage
data power
dependencies
reputation
default risks
bindings
emotional labor
future duties

The RME can capture these things as relations.

Thereby they become tradable, insurable, taxable, regulable and assessable.

That creates a boom.

Second: it makes more things liquid

Many assets are difficult to trade today:

future ability to work
incomplete contracts
small claims
local trust
use rights
care services
data rights
risk reductions
social contributions

The RME can turn them into tradable bundles.

What was previously only latent becomes market power.

Third: it reduces informational blindness

A price says:

This thing costs 100.

A relation table says:

This thing is useful, but risky.
It has an open liability.
It is ecologically burdened.
It depends on three suppliers.
It is accepted by the state.
It can be transferred in two weeks.
It is only partially insured.

That is much richer market information.

Fourth: it can include external costs

Environmental costs, care costs, social damage and risks no longer disappear so easily from the price.

They stand as relations inside the currency itself.

A product can no longer simply be cheap if, in truth, it generates high damage. These damages hang on the product as negative relations.

Fifth: it can coordinate production more finely

Because every enterprise sees not only prices but relationship networks, production can be planned more precisely.

The economy sees:

where labor power is missing
where supply chains break
where risks rise
where resources are unused
where trust is missing
where state permits block activity
where care demand grows
where ecological burdens arise

That is stronger than a market that communicates mainly through prices.

The boom of the RME arises because suddenly everything that was previously illiquid is transformed into tradable relations.

This concerns:

unused abilities
small time windows
trust
data
reputation
future claims
micro-rights
care contributions
risk reduction
social networks
local resources
unused machine time
unused permits

Example:

A craftsperson has not only “labor power”. He or she has a bundle:

skills
tools

local customer relationships
insurances
free time windows
guarantee history

In the normal market economy this becomes roughly:

Hourly rate: 70 euros.

In the RME this becomes a rich, tradable portfolio.

The market can thereby deploy the person more precisely, finance them better, insure them better and connect them better with other resources.

That increases productivity.

But it has a price.

20. The price of the boom

The price is:

privacy
simplicity
innocence
autonomy
non-availability
human dignity
social calm

The RME makes the economy stronger because it reaches deeper into reality.

But the deeper a market reaches into reality, the less remains outside the market.

The classical market economy leaves some things imprecise. That imprecision is sometimes inefficient, but it also protects.

Not everything is captured. Not everything is assessed. Not everything is traded.

The RME removes this protective fuzziness.

That is its power and its danger.

The RME booms because it economizes more reality.
It becomes dangerous because it economizes more humanity.

21. The evil amoral side

The evil version of the RME is called:

It says:

If everything is a relation,

then everything is tradable.

Also:

labor binding
bodily access
right of residence
debt dependency
data identity
reputation
social belonging
care duty
intimacy
political influence
family relationships

That is the dark side.

It cannot simply be defined away. It is logically near if one truly relationalizes everything and leaves it to the market.

The evil version no longer treats human beings as persons, but as bundles of exploitable relations:

ability to work
obedience
debt
body
data
residence status
social contacts
consumption profile
reputation
dependencies

It is amoral because it asks:

How can this relation be exploited?

22. Employers and employees in the evil version

In the legitimate version, labor contracts are traded.

In the evil version, human beings are effectively traded through their dependencies.

Not openly as “sale of people”, but through relational bundles such as:

exclusive labor binding
debt binding
housing binding
visa binding
reputation control
data control
termination penalty
resale of labor claims
control over future income

Then a new form of bondage arises, even if it technically appears as a contract.

For example:

Employee A owes firm B labor performance.
Firm B sells this claim to firm C.
A is not formally sold.
But control over A's future moves.

That is the dangerous point.

The human being is not sold directly, but his or her decisive life relations are sold.

If enough relations are transferred, the difference is practically only legal.

Whoever controls a person's working time, housing, debt, residence right, reputation and data
controls the person.

Therefore it is not enough to say:

We trade only relations, not people.

A human being can be fully dominated through his or her relations.

23. Why governments are mandatory

A purely private RME would morally derail.

Not because all people are evil, but because the market would discover every exploitable relation.

When a relation brings profit, pressure arises to make it tradable.

Therefore the state needs hard boundaries:

This relation may not be sold.
This relation may not be pledged.
This relation may not be transferred.
This relation may not be permanently binding.
This relation may not be used against basic rights.
This relation expires in case of abuse.

Without such boundaries, a market for dependency emerges.

The state must therefore not only correct market failure, but prevent market encroachment.

That is more than regulation. It is civilizational protection.

24. Inalienable relations

The system needs a class of relations that must never become currency.

I call them:

inalienable relations

They include:

Relation Why inalienable
Human dignity must not be an object of exchange
Bodily self-determination protects against sale of the body
Basic rights must not be assigned away
Voting right protects democracy from purchase
Freedom to terminate employment prevents labor bondage
Freedom of movement prevents private imprisonment
Core data protection prevents total personality exploitation
Basic family rights prevents trade in family power
Minimum labor rights prevents exploitation through need
Insolvency discharge prevents eternal debt
Protection against coercion protects genuine consent

These relations must stand outside the market.

The market may see them, but it must not trade them.

25. The worst danger: the human being as portfolio

The extreme evil form is:

Person = portfolio of exploitable relations

Then a human being is assessed according to:

labor power

health
data value
reproductive ability
residence status
network access
consumption profile
political influence
emotional ties

That would be a monstrously efficient system.

It could generate enormous profits because it translates human existence completely into economic availability.

But for precisely that reason it must be rejected.

It would be stronger than a market economy, but in the wrong direction:

stronger in control
stronger in exploitation
stronger in prediction
stronger in utilization
stronger in disguising coercion

Not every strength is good.

A prison can be organizationally stronger than a free marketplace. That does not make it morally better.

26. Data protection as a survival condition

An RME needs data. A lot of data.

Without data, relations cannot be checked.

But this is exactly how surveillance emerges.

Whoever knows all relations of a person knows that person’s economic soul:

debts
work
illness
family
consumption
mistakes
risks
contacts
political patterns
dependencies

This can become a total social-credit system.

Therefore the RME needs:

data minimization
pseudonymization
purpose limitation
deletion rights
private zones
prohibition of certain analyses
right to economic opacity

That sounds paradoxical, but it is necessary:

A relational market needs data.
A free society needs boundaries against data.

right not to be evaluated

This right protects:

friendship

Without this right, the RME becomes a total evaluation machine.

The welfare state must function differently than today.

It does not provide only money transfers, but secure basic relations:

housing protection relation
health relation
education relation
nutrition relation
legal protection relation
mobility relation
digital identity relation
debt protection relation
work transition relation

This means:

A person should not only have a minimum income, but a guaranteed bundle of inalienable basic relations.

For example:

Basic relation Protection
Housing no one falls immediately out of the system
Health illness does not become a total market penalty
Education abilities remain developable
Legal protection contracts can be challenged
Data dignity the profile is not fully exploited
Debt discharge no eternal economic death
Labor freedom no forced binding to an employer

This is necessary because otherwise the RME would mercilessly sort out people with weak relations.

29. Labor market with protection

A legitimate labor market in the RME needs clear rules:

Labor relations are always limited in time.
Labor relations are terminable.
Labor relations are not fully transferable.
Labor relations must not be merged with housing, debt and residence into coercive bundles.
Labor relations contain minimum protection.
Labor relations must not be used against basic rights.

Especially important is the prohibition of toxic bundling.

The danger is not only a labor contract.

The danger is:

labor contract + housing + debt + visa + data profile + reputation control

If all of this lies with one employer, private domination arises.

Therefore the law must break up certain bundles.

30. Antitrust law and power

In the RME, new monopolies arise.

Not only money monopolies, but relation monopolies.

An enterprise can become powerful because it controls:

identity data
payment relations
labor-market access
reputation
contract standards
insurance
supply chains
public interfaces

The most dangerous enterprise is not necessarily the one with the most money, but the one with the greatest control over connectivity.

Therefore a new antitrust law is needed:

No private actor may monopolize central identity relations.
No private actor may solely control reputation access.
No private actor may fully bundle labor, housing, credit and data relations.
No private actor may make people economically unremovable.

31. Insolvency

In a normal economy, insolvency means:

A debtor cannot fulfill money claims.

In the RME, insolvency means:

An actor can no longer fulfill his or her relations.

This can concern:

delivery duties
work commitments
debt relations
guarantees
liabilities
data duties
environmental duties
rental duties
insurance commitments

Insolvency law must therefore not only order debts, but disentangle relations.

Goals:

delete toxic relations
rescue fulfillable relations
lift unfair bindings
protect basic rights
preserve market trust
enable a new start

Without insolvency law, people and firms would remain permanently trapped in old relational debts.

32. Insurance

Insurance is especially important in this system.

Every relation can fail.

Insurable are:

delivery failure
incapacity for work
data loss
reputational damage
breach of contract
environmental damage
machine failure
debtor default
legal dispute
need for care

Insurances themselves become relation bundles.

They make risky relations more tradable.

This increases liquidity.

But there is danger here too:

Whoever controls insurance controls market access.

If someone cannot obtain insurance because of illness, origin, age or social risk, they are relationally excluded.

Therefore public basic insurance is needed.

33. Foreign trade

Between states there are different relational standards.

One country might assess labor relations differently than another.

Therefore foreign trade needs:

relation translation
recognition of rights
labor standards
environmental standards
data standards
arbitration courts
sanction rules

An export is not only an export of goods, but an export of a relation bundle:

product
delivery guarantee
liability
environmental balance
working conditions
data rights
customs relation
currency acceptance

This could make global supply chains more honest.

But it could also organize global exploitation more precisely.

34. Environment

The RME is ecologically potentially stronger than the market economy because environmental damage no longer has to remain merely external costs.

A product carries its environmental relations with it:

energy consumption
emissions
soil damage
water consumption
repairability
recycling duty
biodiversity damage
compensation duties
liabilities

Then a cheap product is not simply cheap if it contains ecological debts.

It can count as “relationally burdened”.

That is a major advantage.

But here, too, there is an evil side:

Rich actors buy clean relations.
Poor regions carry dirty relations.
Environmental burdens are securitized and exported.

Therefore prohibitions are needed, not only markets.

Some environmental damage must not be tradable.

35. Education and abilities

Education becomes a central form of capital in the RME.

An ability is not a thing, but a relation:

Person can fulfill task X under conditions Y with quality Z.

This can be certified.

Advantage:

Abilities become more precisely visible.
People can find better work.
Enterprises can hire more suitably.
Further training becomes easier to finance.

Danger:

People are reduced to ability profiles.
Old mistakes remain in the table.
Non-certified abilities become invisible.
Education becomes a total utilization machine.

Therefore it needs:

right to reassessment
right to be forgotten
public education
prohibition of discriminatory ability profiles

Dangerous would be markets for:

voting rights
political loyalty
renunciation of protest

party access

Therefore it must apply:

The state must prevent economic relations from devouring democratic relations.

38. The basic law of the RME

A good RME needs a highest principle:

Everything economic may be captured relationally,
but not everything relational may be traded economically.

This is the most important sentence.

Without it, the system becomes evil.

With it, it can be strong without becoming totalitarian.

39. Good version of the system

The good version looks like this:

Currency = tradable relation bundles
Humans = inalienable persons
Market = matching of rights, duties and resources
State = boundary setter and stability guarantor
Welfare state = provider of safe basic relations
Enterprises = productive relation nodes
Banks = relation transformers
Central bank = quality and recognition authority
Law = protection against toxic tradability

This version is stronger than a market economy because it brings more reality into the market.

But it protects human dignity through hard non-market zones.

40. Evil version of the system

The evil version looks like this:

Currency = all relations
Humans = exploitable portfolios
Labor = tradable binding
Debt = control instrument
Data = personality access
Reputation = disciplinary power
State = weak or bought
Enterprises = private domination nodes
Market = total exploitation

This version probably creates a brutal boom.

Why?

Because it mobilizes everything:

every ability
every debt
every weakness
every binding
every future
every intimacy
every dependency
every social relationship

But this boom is morally poisoned.

It is strong like a machine that also burns human beings as fuel.

41. The special economic boom

The boom would be especially strong for five reasons:

1. More securities

People and firms could use much more as security:

future revenues
work commitments
data rights
supply chains
reputation
insurances
use rights

This makes credit capacity explode.

2. More liquidity

Things that were previously stuck become tradable:

small claims
time windows
local claims
micro-services
incomplete rights

3. More matching

Supply and demand are linked more precisely.

Not only:

I need work.

But:

I need this skill,
for this duration,
under these risks,
with this insurance,
in this region,
with this legal quality.

4. More control

Enterprises can steer processes far more precisely.

This increases efficiency.

5. More future utilization

The future becomes more tradable.

Future work, future deliveries, future data, future payments and future uses are economically utilized already today.

That creates growth.

But it can push people and societies into debt far into the future.

42. The negative price of the boom

The boom costs:

more surveillance
more contractual dependency
more complexity
more power for platforms
more pressure toward self-exploitation
more risk of debt bondage
fewer private life spaces
less spontaneous freedom
less non-evaluability

In the classical market economy, someone can be poor, but their entire life is not automatically visible as a tradable table.

In the RME, the threat is:

Every weakness becomes a relation.
Every relation becomes a risk.
Every risk becomes a price.
Every price becomes a market barrier.

That can become brutal.

43. Example: the dangerous life data set

A person in a bad RME could appear like this:

Area Relation Market effect

Family care duty lower availability
Place of residence weak infrastructure lower labor connectivity
Data negative consumption profile worse offers

The system would be extremely informative.

But it could pin people down.

Therefore rights are needed to:

new beginning
deletion
context separation
objection
human review
non-discrimination

44. How to tame the evil side

It cannot be magicked away. It can only be institutionally tamed.

Needed are:

Problem Countermeasure
Trade in human relations inalienability law
Labor bondage termination right, maximum binding, minimum protection
Debt slavery insolvency, debt cut, interest limits
Data domination data protection, deletion, purpose limitation
Platform monopolies antitrust law, interoperability
Reputation prison right to reassessment
Social exclusion public basic relations
Toxic bundling disentanglement law
Political purchasability ban on tradable citizen rights
Ecological outsourcing non-tradable environmental limits

The state must therefore not only set market rules. It must determine which relations are marketable at all.

45. The constitution of the RME

A complete RME needs a kind of economic constitution.

It could read as follows:

Article 1: Protection of persons

Persons are not tradable relation carriers.
Only voluntary, limited and revocable performances may be marketable.

Article 2: Inalienability

Basic rights, bodily freedom, political rights and core identity are inalienable.

Article 3: Transparency

Every tradable relation must disclose origin, risk, duty and counterparty.

Article 4: Deletion

Fulfilled, abusive or outdated relations must be deletable or correctable.

Article 5: Disentanglement

No actor may fully control a person's work, housing, credit, data, insurance and residence right.

Article 6: State basic relations

Every person has a claim to minimum safe relations for life, law, health, education and participation.

Article 7: Market boundaries

Whatever structurally destroys a person's freedom must not serve as currency.

46. Accounting

Accounting in the RME is no longer only double-entry bookkeeping in money amounts.

It is relational balance-sheeting.

A household or enterprise has:

Active relations:
- claims
- rights
- securities
- uses
- receivables
- guarantees

Passive relations:
- duties
- debts
- liabilities
- risks
- dependencies
- environmental burdens

Balance means:

Which relations strengthen me?
Which relations burden me?
Which relations are transferable?
Which relations are dangerous?
Which relations expire soon?
Which relations are disputed?

An enterprise can look rich but be relationally weak if it carries many risks and dependencies.

Another can have little money but be relationally strong because it owns stable, productive and trustworthy relationships.

47. Measuring growth

Gross domestic product is not enough.

The RME measures growth as improvement in the national stock of relations.

Important indicators would be:

productive relations
stable labor relations
healthy credit relations
low toxic dependencies
ecological regeneration relations
education relations
trust density
social protection
innovation relations
supply-chain stability

A society does not grow only when more is sold.

It grows when its relations become more sustainable, freer, more productive and less toxic.

48. Crisis

A crisis in the RME can take various forms:

Trust crisis

Nobody knows anymore which relations are valid.

Fulfillment crisis

Many actors cannot fulfill their duties.

Data crisis

Proofs are manipulated or stolen.

Moral crisis

Too many people are degraded by tradable relations.

Liquidity crisis

Everyone wants only state-guaranteed relations.

Power crisis

Some platforms control central relation nodes.

The state must then:

freeze relations
split toxic bundles
provide public guarantees
delete debts
secure minimum supply
break monopolies
correct data
create legal clarity

49. Why normal market economy is weaker by comparison

The normal market economy is weaker because it simplifies heavily.

It says:

Everything has a price.

The RME says:

Everything has a structure.

The normal market economy often recognizes too late:

Supply chains break.
Labor is exploited.
The environment is destroyed.
Data power grows.
Debts become toxic.
People become dependent.

The RME can make these things visible earlier because they appear as relations inside the currency itself.

That makes it analytically, organizationally and financially stronger.

But the price is that the system penetrates deeper into society.

50. The decisive contradiction

The RME wants to make everything visible.

Freedom, however, needs areas that are not fully visible and not fully tradable.

This creates the basic conflict:

The more economically complete the system is,
the more humanly dangerous it becomes.

A perfect relational market machine would probably be inhuman.

A human RME must therefore be incomplete.

It must consciously allow blind spots:

privacy
non-evaluation
non-transferability
forgiveness
new beginning
unpaid dignity
unsellable rights

This is not a weakness. It is the condition of its legitimacy.

The relational monetary economy is stronger than a market economy because it does not reduce the market to prices.

It can see more:

risks
dependencies
trust
future
data
environment
power

It can trade more:

claims
uses
securities
labor performances
insurances
data rights
supply chains
reputation
ecological duties

It can steer more:

production
credit
risk
social policy
environmental damage
labor markets
public goods

That is why it creates a special economic boom.

But this boom has a dark reverse side:

Everything that becomes visible can be assessed.
Everything that is assessed can be traded.
Everything that is traded can become power over human beings.

Therefore the central political task is not merely to make this system efficient.

The central task is:

to decide which relations must never become currency.

The best version of the RME would be an economy that is more complex, more honest and more productive than the normal market economy.

The worst version would be a total market in which people are not officially sold, but are practically ruled through their labor, debt, data, housing, reputation and dependency relations.

The difference between both versions is not technology.

The difference is law.

And deeper still:

The difference is the decision
whether the market serves the human being
or the human being, as relation, belongs to the market.

# Stacked Labor-Block Economy (SLBE)

## A complete economic system based on a labor currency

This document describes an economic system in which currency is created from verified labor. Labor is understood as the transformation of input units into output units. The sum of these units forms the labor quantity. This labor quantity is multiplied by value factors for different types of work and accumulated as labor currency.

The central idea is:

> Currency is not created from gold, debt, or arbitrary decree alone, but from useful, verifiable, and non-duplicated labor.

The system separates five layers:

1. **Labor quantity** — how many units were moved, processed, or created?
2. **Type of work** — what kind of labor was performed?
3. **Labor value** — how much labor currency is created?
4. **Price** — how much does the result cost in the market?
5. **Ownership and claims** — who owns the result, the payment, and the usage rights?

---

# 1. Basic axioms

## 1.1 Everything economic consists of blocks

For economic purposes, the universe is divided into **blocks**. A block can be:

- an area,
- a spatial volume,
- a material quantity,
- a time quantity,
- an energy unit,
- an information structure,
- a communication process,
- a mathematical relationship,
- a causal relationship,
- a product,
- a service,
- repaired damage,
- a prevented crisis.

The economy measures not status or mere intention, but verifiable changes of blocks.

## 1.2 Labor is transformation

Labor means:

```text
Input -> Transformation -> Output
```

Labor takes input units and creates output units.

$$
E = \sum E_i
$$

$$
O = \sum O_j
$$

Where:

- $E$ = sum of input units,
- $O$ = sum of output units.

The raw labor quantity is:

$$
M = E + O
$$

## 1.3 Labor is stacked

Every valid work process creates a **labor stack**. A product, service, or knowledge object therefore has a history of previous labor blocks.

Example: A house contains raw-material work, transport work, area work, 3D construction work, planning, communication, mathematical structural analysis, causal coordination, and maintenance.

The labor stack is the economic genealogy of an object.

## 1.4 Value is labor quantity multiplied by a work factor

Labor quantity alone is not enough. Different types of work have different systemic leverage.

$$
LC = M \cdot K
$$

Where:

- $LC$ = labor currency,
- $M$ = labor quantity,
- $K$ = value factor of the work type.

## 1.5 Price is not identical to labor currency

Labor value is created by work. Market price also depends on demand, scarcity, risk, quality, and property rights.

A basic price formula is:

$$
P = (\alpha E + \beta O) \cdot K \cdot Q \cdot S
$$

Where:

- $P$ = price in labor currency,
- $\alpha$ = weight of input units,
- $\beta$ = weight of output units,
- $K$ = work factor,
- $Q$ = quality factor,
- $S$ = scarcity or demand factor.

---

# 2. The currency

The currency is called:

```text
LC = Labor Currency
```

One unit, $1 LC$, corresponds to one verified basic labor block of arable agriculture with factor $1$, standard quality, and real usefulness.

Arable agriculture is the reference point:

$$
1 \text{ basic labor} = 1 LC
$$

All other types of work are valued relative to this baseline.

---

# 3. Value factors of labor

Arable agriculture is the base with factor $1$.

| Type of work | Value factor |
|---|---:|
| Arable agriculture / basic labor | $1\times$ |
| Area work | $2\times$ |
| 3D work / house construction | $3\times$ |
| Time-critical work | $4\times$ |
| Causally important work | $5\times$ |
| Mental work | $6\times$ |
| Communication work | $7\times$ |
| Dividing the universe into blocks | $8\times$ |
| Mathematics | $10\times$ |

These factors do not mean that people have different human worth. They mean that certain types of work have higher systemic leverage.

---

# 4. The types of work

## 4.1 Arable agriculture — factor 1

Arable agriculture produces food, raw biological materials, and basic biological provision.

$$
K = 1
$$

It is not unimportant; it is the baseline. If food becomes scarce, its market price rises through the scarcity factor $S$.

## 4.2 Area work — factor 2

Area work processes two-dimensional spaces:

- structuring fields,
- preparing road surfaces,
- surveying land,
- organizing solar installation areas,
- organizing storage areas,
- producing maps,
- territorial planning.

$$
K = 2
$$

## 4.3 3D work / house construction — factor 3

3D work changes volumes and spatial structures:

- house construction,
- machine construction,
- bridge construction,
- furniture construction,
- pipe systems,
- warehouses,
- infrastructure bodies,
- physical constructions.

$$
K = 3
$$

## 4.4 Time-critical work — factor 4

Time-critical work is work whose value strongly depends on when it happens.

Examples:

- emergency medicine,
- firefighting,
- rescue work,
- crisis repair,
- deadline-critical logistics,
- harvesting before a storm,
- security response,
- server rescue during an outage.

$$
K = 4
$$

Work is time-critical only if a real time window exists. Artificially created urgency must not count.

## 4.5 Causally important work — factor 5

Causally important work influences many later processes.

Examples:

- a decision that enables many other works,
- repair of a central system,
- planning on which many people depend,
- removal of a bottleneck,
- system diagnosis,
- central coordination.

$$
K = 5
$$

Work is causally important only if this is verifiable:

```text
Without this work, other work would have been blocked, degraded, or impossible.
```

## 4.6 Mental work — factor 6

Mental work creates models, decisions, ideas, plans, strategies, or insights.

Examples:

- research,
- planning,
- design,
- analysis,
- lesson preparation,
- diagnosis,
- strategy,
- technical conception.

$$
K = 6
$$

For currency creation, mental work needs a verifiable output:

- text,
- plan,
- model,
- decision,
- explanation,
- draft,
- diagnosis,
- solution.

## 4.7 Communication work — factor 7

Communication work changes the information state of other people or systems.

Examples:

- coordination,
- teaching,
- negotiation,
- translation,
- leadership,
- moderation,
- crisis communication,
- knowledge transfer,
- interface work.

$$
K = 7
$$

Communication counts as labor only if it achieves at least one of the following:

- someone understands something better,
- a decision becomes possible,
- an error is avoided,
- a conflict is resolved,
- multiple actors are coordinated,
- a process is accelerated,
- knowledge is reliably transferred.

Spam, manipulation, and empty self-display do not count.

## 4.8 Dividing the universe into blocks — factor 8

This work creates measurability.

Examples:

- classifications,
- mappings,
- taxonomies,
- standards,
- data models,
- measurement systems,
- ontologies,
- digital twins,
- resource registers,
- production grids,
- causal graphs,
- simulation structures.

$$
K = 8
$$

This work is valuable because it makes other work comparable, plannable, and computable.

## 4.9 Mathematics — factor 10

Mathematics is the highest normal form of work in this system.

It creates:

- proofs,
- equations,
- optimizations,
- algorithms,
- models,
- forecasts,
- abstractions,
- formal structures,
- calculation methods,
- decision systems.

$$
K = 10
$$

Mathematics counts as factor 10 only if it is verifiable. Valid mathematical work needs at least one of the following:

- formal proof,
- traceable calculation,
- tested algorithm,
- correct model,
- successfully applied optimization,
- verifiable usefulness in other processes.

False mathematics creates no value. Dangerously false mathematics creates negative value.

---

# 5. Stacking rule for combined work

Many jobs have multiple properties. A mathematical optimization for rescue logistics can be mathematical, time-critical, causally important, mental, and communicative at the same time.

Multiplying all factors would create extreme values:

$$
10 \cdot 4 \cdot 5 \cdot 6 \cdot 7 = 8400
$$

Therefore, this system uses the additive stacking rule:

$$
K = 1 + \sum (k_i - 1)
$$

Example: mathematics + time-critical + causally important:

$$
K = 1 + (10-1) + (4-1) + (5-1)
$$

$$
K = 17
$$

The work is therefore worth $17\times$ basic labor, not $8400\times$.

---

# 6. Segmentation instead of label abuse

Work must not automatically receive all high factors. A house construction project is not entirely mathematics just because a structural engineer performed calculations.

Therefore each project is divided into work segments.

Example: house construction:

| Segment | Possible factor |
|---|---:|
| Material transport | $1$ to $3$ |
| Land surveying | $2$ or $8$ |
| Foundation work | $3$ |
| Masonry | $3$ |
| Structural calculation | $10$ |
| Construction coordination | $7$ |
| Time-critical repair | $4$ additional |
| Causally central decision | $5$ additional |

The total value is:

$$
LC_{project} = \sum_s M_s \cdot K_s \cdot Q_s
$$

Only each specific segment receives its appropriate factor.

---

# 7. Quality factor

Not all work of the same type has the same quality. Therefore the quality factor $Q$ exists.

| Quality | Factor $Q$ |
|---|---:|
| Invalid / harmful | $0$ or negative |
| Poor | $0.5$ |
| Acceptable | $0.8$ |
| Standard | $1.0$ |
| Good | $1.2$ |
| Very good | $1.5$ |
| Exceptional, systemically proven | up to $2.0$ |

Basic formula:

$$
LC = M \cdot K \cdot Q
$$

High factors reward only valid work.

---

# 8. Input units and output units

Input and output units are weighted differently:

$$
P = (\alpha E + \beta O) \cdot K
$$

A useful default setting:

$$
\alpha = 0.5
$$

$$
\beta = 1.0
$$

Inputs count, but outputs count more because output is directly usable.

Example:

$$
E = 40, \quad O = 100, \quad K = 3
$$

$$
P = (0.5 \cdot 40 + 1 \cdot 100) \cdot 3 = 360
$$

The labor quantity would be:

$$
M = 40 + 100 = 140
$$

The labor value would be:

$$
LC = 140 \cdot 3 = 420
$$

Price and labor value are related, but not identical.

---

# 9. Three value layers

Each product has three values.

## 9.1 Raw labor quantity

$$
M = E + O
$$

## 9.2 Weighted labor value

$$
W = M \cdot K \cdot Q
$$

## 9.3 Market price

$$
P = (\alpha E + \beta O) \cdot K \cdot Q \cdot S
$$

The scarcity factor $S$ allows shortages or oversupply to change prices.

---

# 10. Scarcity factor

Even simple work can become expensive if its result is scarce.

| Market situation | $S$ |
|---|---:|
| Oversupply | $0.7$ |
| Balanced | $1.0$ |
| Scarce | $1.5$ |
| Critically scarce | $2.5$ |
| Life-critical crisis | $4.0$ or rationing |

This protects essential basic work such as food production.

---

# 11. Money creation

New labor currency must not be created arbitrarily. It is created only by verified, useful, and non-duplicated labor.

## 11.1 Private commissioned work

A person or company commissions work. The client pays existing LC to the worker. No new currency is created.

## 11.2 Public or communal work

Some work has no single buyer but high usefulness for everyone. Examples:

- road mapping,
- mathematical foundations,
- public safety,
- disaster protection,
- educational infrastructure,
- preventive healthcare,
- open standards,
- dividing the universe into blocks.

This work is paid from a public labor fund. New LC may be created here, but only against verified public usefulness.

## 11.3 Inventory production

Production for storage can be financed through a provisional labor credit. This credit is backed by the product. When the product is sold, the credit is repaid or destroyed. If it is not sold, the producer bears the loss.

---

# 12. No double counting

A labor block that has already been paid must not be paid again in full as new work.

A stone that has already been produced carries its old labor stack into a house. During house construction, only the new work counts:

- transport,
- processing,
- fitting,
- combining,
- new structure,
- new output.

Formally:

$$
Total\ stack = old\ stack + new\ labor\ block
$$

Not:

$$
Total\ stack = old\ stack + old\ stack\ again + new\ labor\ block
$$

---

# 13. The labor account

Each participant has a labor account with four parts.

## 13.1 Payment balance

Available labor currency:

$$
LC_{free}
$$

## 13.2 Labor register

Personal work history:

$$
LR = \sum verified\ labor\ blocks
$$

The labor register is reputation and proof, not merely money.

## 13.3 Ownership register

Claims to products, tools, buildings, usage rights, company shares, intellectual works, and land-use licenses.

## 13.4 Obligation register

Loans, delivery obligations, maintenance duties, compensation claims, contractual penalties, and public charges.

---

# 14. Actors

## 14.1 Individuals

They offer labor, consume goods, learn skills, and hold labor accounts.

## 14.2 Households

Households combine consumption, care, housing, and resources.

## 14.3 Firms

Firms combine labor, capital, raw materials, organization, knowledge, communication, and mathematics.

## 14.4 Cooperatives

Cooperatives belong to workers or users. Profit is distributed according to contribution, risk, and rules.

## 14.5 Public institutions

They manage standards, verification, infrastructure, resource rights, safety, education, health, law, and monetary stability.

## 14.6 Verifiers

Verifiers check:

- Was the work actually done?
- Is the output real?
- Is the quality correct?
- Is the factor justified?
- Was anything counted twice?
- Are there negative side effects?

---

# 15. Markets

## 15.1 Goods market

Physical goods are sold: food, tools, clothing, houses, machines, energy, and raw materials.

$$
P_{good} = labor\ stack + resource\ rent + risk + scarcity
$$

## 15.2 Service market

Direct labor is sold: repair, care, consulting, teaching, transport, planning, and communication.

$$
P_{service} = (\alpha E + \beta O) \cdot K \cdot Q \cdot S
$$

## 15.3 Knowledge market

Models, algorithms, blueprints, research reports, simulations, optimizations, and standards are traded.

Knowledge has a special property: the first creation is expensive, the copy is cheap. Therefore, the creator receives a high initial value and time-limited usage claims. After that, knowledge gradually moves into the commons.

## 15.4 Labor market

People offer work segments.

$$
Wage = LC \cdot S
$$

## 15.5 Capital market

Capital is stored previous labor: machines, tools, buildings, software, data models, patents, and inventories.

Capital return consists of depreciation replacement, maintenance replacement, risk premium, and productivity share.

## 15.6 Resource market

Natural resources were not created by labor. Land, water, air, mineral deposits, radio frequencies, and ecosystems are managed through usage rights and resource rents.

$$
R = scarcity \cdot usage\ quantity \cdot ecological\ cost
$$

---

# 16. Ownership

## 16.1 Labor ownership

Whoever performs work initially owns the claim to its value.

## 16.2 Product ownership

Whoever creates or lawfully buys a product owns it.

## 16.3 Usage ownership

Some things can be used but not absolutely owned: land, water rights, public infrastructure, frequencies, and ecological spaces.

## 16.4 Knowledge ownership

Knowledge receives authorship claims and time-limited exploitation rights. Later it can move into open licenses or the commons.

---

# 17. Firms and profit distribution

A firm creates output through many labor blocks.

$$
Surplus = revenue - costs
$$

Costs consist of inputs, wages, capital depreciation, resource rents, taxes, and risk.

Surplus is distributed according to:

1. labor contribution,
2. capital risk,
3. organizational contribution,
4. innovation contribution,
5. community contribution.

A fair default formula:

$$
Share_i = \frac{Contribution_i}{\sum Contribution}
$$

---

# 18. Taxes and common fund

## 18.1 Transaction levy

$$
T = \tau \cdot P
$$

Example: $\tau = 5\%$.

## 18.2 Resource rent

Whoever uses scarce natural resources pays. These revenues belong to the community.

## 18.3 Negative-output levy

Whoever creates damage pays: pollution, misinformation, production defects, safety risks, or blocking other work.

## 18.4 High-factor levy

Highly valued work uses many social prerequisites: education, language, infrastructure, data, standards, and previous mathematics. A small leverage levy can limit extreme inequality.

---

# 19. Citizen dividend and basic provision

Because natural resources and public standards belong to everyone, every citizen receives a basic dividend or basic provision.

It can consist of:

- basic food,
- basic energy,
- basic housing,
- education,
- healthcare access,
- digital access,
- public transport,
- a small LC payment.

This protects children, the elderly, the sick, caregivers, learners, and people in transition.

---

# 20. Negative work

Not all work is good. Effort can create damage.

Examples:

- false structural analysis,
- bad medicine,
- disinformation,
- unnecessary bureaucracy,
- environmental destruction,
- faulty mathematics,
- poor block decomposition,
- sabotaging communication.

Negative output units:

$$
O^- = damage
$$

Effective labor quantity:

$$
M_{effective} = E + O^+ - \gamma O^-
$$

If damage exceeds usefulness, no labor currency is created; instead debt or liability arises.

---

# 21. Verification

Every valued work process needs proof.

A labor block contains:

| Field | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Actor | Who worked? |
| Time | When was the work done? |
| Place / system | Where or on what? |
| Input units | What was used? |
| Output units | What was created? |
| Factor | Which type of work? |
| Quality | How good was the output? |
| Usefulness | Who or what benefits? |
| Damage | Which side effects? |
| Verifier | Who confirms it? |
| Labor stack | Which previous work is included? |

Without verification, there is no valid LC claim.

---

# 22. Factor verification

High factors require special proof.

| Factor | Proof |
|---|---|
| Time-critical $4$ | Deadline, delay risk, reaction time, prevented damage |
| Causally important $5$ | Causal graph, dependent processes, bottleneck removal, counterfactual |
| Mental $6$ | Document, plan, model, diagnosis, draft, decision, explanation |
| Communication $7$ | Receiver, understood information, coordinated action, resolved conflict |
| Block decomposition $8$ | Classification, measurement scheme, map, standard, data model |
| Mathematics $10$ | Proof, calculation, algorithm, simulation, forecasting model, formal check |

---

# 23. Education system

Education is crucial because it moves people into higher work factors. Education often contains mental work, communication work, mathematics, and block decomposition.

Teaching is highly valued when it creates real competence growth. Learners do not automatically receive high factors, but they can receive educational grants because society later benefits from higher abilities.

---

# 24. Healthcare system

Healthcare work is often highly valued.

| Activity | Possible factors |
|---|---|
| Care work | $1$, $6$, $7$ |
| Emergency medicine | $4$, $5$, $6$ |
| Diagnosis | $6$, sometimes $10$ |
| Surgical coordination | $4$, $5$, $7$ |
| Medical research | $6$, $8$, $10$ |

Healthcare work receives high values when it saves lives, prevents follow-up damage, or has time-critical and causally central effects.

---

# 25. Infrastructure

Infrastructure contains area work, 3D work, causally important work, communication, mathematical planning, and block decomposition.

A bridge is not just concrete. It is a causal node in a transport network. If it enables many other works, it receives causal value.

---

# 26. Agriculture

Arable agriculture has factor $1$, but remains system-critical.

Protection mechanisms:

1. scarcity factor,
2. basic provision,
3. land rent.

Farmers are therefore not automatically paid poorly. Market conditions, scarcity, and public provision can greatly increase their income.

---

# 27. Mathematics as top-level work

Mathematics receives factor $10$, but under strict control.

## 27.1 Pure mathematics

Proofs, structures, and theories.

## 27.2 Applied mathematics

Optimization, simulation, cryptography, planning, statistics, and machine control.

## 27.3 System mathematics

Mathematics that improves other economic parts: production planning, logistics, risk models, energy distribution, and material savings.

Mathematics plus causal importance:

$$
K = 1 + (10-1) + (5-1) = 14
$$

---

# 28. Communication work

Communication work with factor $7$ is a high sector because complex economies often fail due to poor coordination.

Communication creates value when it transfers knowledge, prevents misunderstandings, enables decisions, builds trust, or resolves conflicts.

It is not counted by the number of words, but by successful state change in the receiver.

---

# 29. Block decomposition of the universe

Factor $8$ is the foundation of the whole economic simulation. Whoever defines blocks creates measurability.

Examples:

- dividing a field into zones,
- dividing a building into components,
- dividing a company into processes,
- dividing a supply chain into nodes,
- dividing a mathematical problem into variables,
- dividing a city into functional spaces,
- dividing a social problem into causes.

False block decomposition is dangerous because it creates false prices, false planning, and false policy.

---

# 30. Credit system

## 30.1 Labor advance

A person receives LC before the work is completed. If the work succeeds, the advance is offset. If it fails, debt or loss arises.

## 30.2 Production credit

A firm receives LC to buy inputs. The credit is backed by inventory, machines, expected output, contracts, or insurance.

## 30.3 Research option

Research, mathematics, and block decomposition can have uncertain usefulness. Therefore they are initially paid only in part. If usefulness appears later, additional LC is released.

---

# 31. Banks and clearing houses

Banks are not free money creators. They are verifiers, risk assessors, credit brokers, trustees, project financiers, and clearing houses.

Provisional LC may be issued only when collateral exists: future work, contract, product, capital, or public guarantee.

---

# 32. Inflation and stability

Four stability rules:

1. Only verified work counts.
2. No double counting.
3. Public money creation is limited.
4. An optional circulation fee prevents permanent hoarding.

Circulation fee:

$$
LC_{t+1} = LC_t \cdot (1-d)
$$

Typical value:

$$
d = 0.001 \text{ to } 0.005
$$

---

# 33. Macro control

The economy continuously measures:

- labor quantity,
- issued LC,
- price level,
- scarcities,
- unemployment,
- resource condition,
- productivity,
- quality errors,
- money velocity.

During inflation: less public LC issuance, stricter verification, higher resource levies, and more production in scarce areas.

During deflation: more public contracts, infrastructure work, education investment, and research funding.

---

# 34. Economic tick of a simulation

One tick runs as follows:

1. Calculate household needs.
2. Generate demand.
3. Determine scarcities.
4. Actors choose work according to ability, price, and need.
5. Reserve inputs.
6. Create labor blocks.
7. Create outputs.
8. Verify quality.
9. Verify factors.
10. Calculate prices.
11. Execute payments.
12. Pay public work from the common fund.
13. Update credits.
14. Update resource stocks.
15. Book damages and negative outputs.
16. Measure inflation and price level.
17. Distribute taxes, rents, and dividends.
18. Update skills and reputation.

---

# 35. Examples

## 35.1 Arable work

Given:

$$
E = 60, \quad O = 140, \quad K = 1, \quad Q = 1
$$

Labor quantity:

$$
M = 60 + 140 = 200
$$

Labor value:

$$
LC = 200 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 = 200
$$

Price:

$$
P = (0.5 \cdot 60 + 1 \cdot 140) \cdot 1 = 170
$$

If food is scarce:

$$
S = 1.5
$$

$$
P = 170 \cdot 1.5 = 255
$$

## 35.2 House construction

| Segment | Quantity $M$ | Factor $K$ | Value |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Area preparation | 200 | 2 | 400 |
| Foundation | 300 | 3 | 900 |
| 3D construction | 1000 | 3 | 3000 |
| Structural analysis | 80 | 10 | 800 |
| Construction coordination | 120 | 7 | 840 |
| Quality inspection | 60 | 6 | 360 |

Total value:

$$
LC = 400 + 900 + 3000 + 800 + 840 + 360 = 6400
$$

## 35.3 Time-critical repair

A repair at an electricity node prevents production loss.

Base 3D/infrastructure:

$$
K = 3
$$

Additionally time-critical and causally important:

$$
K = 1 + (3-1) + (4-1) + (5-1) = 10
$$

For $M = 100$:

$$
LC = 100 \cdot 10 = 1000
$$

## 35.4 Mathematical optimization

A mathematician develops an algorithm that reduces material consumption.

$$
E = 30, \quad O = 80, \quad M = 110
$$

Mathematics:

$$
K = 10
$$

Partly causally important:

$$
K = 12
$$

Labor value:

$$
LC = 110 \cdot 12 = 1320
$$

Price:

$$
P = (0.5 \cdot 30 + 1 \cdot 80) \cdot 12 = 1140
$$

## 35.5 Communication work

A coordinator prevents a production defect through clear coordination.

$$
M = 50, \quad K = 7, \quad Q = 1.2
$$

$$
LC = 50 \cdot 7 \cdot 1.2 = 420
$$

Unnecessary meetings without effect would have $Q = 0$.

# 36. Contract types

## 36.1 Quantity contract

Payment is based on labor quantity:

$$
M = E + O
$$

## 36.2 Result contract

Payment is based on output.

## 36.3 Impact contract

Payment is based on causal usefulness.

Suitable for medicine, safety, optimization, consulting, research, and communication.

## 36.4 Research contract

One part is paid immediately, another later after proven usefulness.

## 36.5 Emergency contract

Time-critical work receives higher factors, but with later review.

---

# 37. Resources and ecology

Every natural stock has:

$$
Stock_{t+1} = Stock_t + Regeneration_t - Extraction_t - Damage_t
$$

If extraction exceeds regeneration, resource rent rises.

Environmental repair counts as work. Environmental damage counts as negative output.

---

# 38. Automation and machines

Machines create output, but their value is distributed among:

- developers,
- machine builders,
- maintainers,
- energy providers,
- owners with risk,
- public infrastructure,
- mathematical and technical preliminary work.

Automation lowers the price of simple labor and increases the importance of mathematics, maintenance, system control, communication, and block decomposition.

---

# 39. Foreign trade

The exchange rate of LC is based on a basket of goods:

$$
1 LC = labor\ value\ of\ a\ basic\ goods\ basket
$$

Imported goods receive an estimated labor stack:

$$
LC_{import} = estimated\ embedded\ labor + transport + customs + scarcity
$$

---

# 40. Social mobility

The system must not trap people permanently in factor-1 work. Therefore the common fund finances:

- education,
- basic mathematical training,
- communication training,
- technical training,
- tool access,
- business loans,
- public data and standards.

Goal:

```text
People should be able to move into higher work factors.
```

---

# 41. Limiting power

High factors such as $7$, $8$, and $10$ create system power. Protection rules:

1. transparency,
2. mandatory verification,
3. no eternal monopoly,
4. public standards,
5. counter-review.

---

# 42. Fraud and protection

| Fraud | Description | Protection |
|---|---|---|
| Circular work | The same object is moved again and again | Origin register |
| Factor fraud | Normal work is labeled as mathematics | Verification |
| Communication spam | Many words without effect | Receiver and usefulness proof |
| Fake mathematics | False models | Formal verification |
| Artificial urgency | Self-created emergencies | Cause review |
| Double counting | Old work is recalculated | Labor-stack register |
| Resource hoarding | Land is blocked | Land rent |
| Quality fraud | Poor output is rated highly | Liability and verifier liability |

---

# 43. Verifier liability

Verifiers must not be able to issue false ratings without consequences. If a verifier rates bad work too highly, they share responsibility.

$$
Liability_{verifier} > 0
$$

# 44. Political order

## 44.1 Labor council

Maintains factor order and standards.

## 44.2 Resource council

Manages land, water, energy, ecology, and usage rights.

# 45. Formal overall model

For a project $p$ with segments $s$:

$$
LC_p = \sum_{s=1}^{n} M_s \cdot K_s \cdot Q_s \cdot U_s
$$

$$
M_s = E_s + O_s
$$

$$
K_s = 1 + \sum (k_{s,i} - 1)
$$

The price:

$$
P_p = \sum_{s=1}^{n}(\alpha E_s + \beta O_s) \cdot K_s \cdot Q_s \cdot S_s
$$

The labor stack of a product:

$$
Stack_p = Stack_{Inputs} + LC_{new}
$$

The free money supply:

$$
G_t = G_{t-1} + LC_{public\ new} + LC_{credit\ new} - LC_{return} - LC_{destruction}
$$

# 47. Simulation algorithm

```text
For each period:

1. Calculate household needs.
2. Generate demand for goods and services.
3. Determine scarcities.
4. Actors choose work according to ability, price, and need.
5. Reserve inputs.
6. Create labor blocks.
7. Create outputs.
8. Verify quality.
9. Verify factors.
10. Calculate prices.
11. Execute payments.
12. Pay public work from the common fund.
13. Update credits.
14. Update resource stocks.
15. Book damages and negative outputs.
16. Measure inflation and price level.
17. Distribute taxes, rents, and dividends.
18. Update skills and reputation.
```

# 50. Greatest danger

The greatest danger is bureaucracy. If every work process must be checked too heavily, the system suffocates.

Therefore three verification layers are needed:

1. **Small work:** quick checks, simple standards.
2. **Medium work:** normal documentation.
3. **High-factor work:** strict verification.

# 51. Final formulas

Most important labor value formula:

$$
\boxed{LC = \sum_s (E_s + O_s) \cdot K_s \cdot Q_s \cdot U_s}
$$

Most important price formula:

$$
\boxed{P = \sum_s (\alpha E_s + \beta O_s) \cdot K_s \cdot Q_s \cdot S_s}

Most important protection:

$$
\boxed{No\ verification \Rightarrow no\ valid\ labor\ currency}
$$

Most important anti-inflation rule:

$$
\boxed{No\ double\ counting\ of\ already\ stacked\ labor}
$$

# 52. Short version as an economic law

> Labor currency is created from verified transformation of input units into output units. The raw labor quantity is the sum of inputs and outputs. Labor value results from labor quantity, work factor, quality, and usefulness. Prices arise from input value, output value, work factor, quality, and scarcity. Natural resources are governed through usage rights and resource rents. High mental, communicative, mathematical, and block-forming work is strongly rewarded, but only when its effect is proven. Labor that has already been paid must not be created again as new currency.

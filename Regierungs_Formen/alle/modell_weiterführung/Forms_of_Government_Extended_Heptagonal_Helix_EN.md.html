Extended Heptagonal Helix of Forms of Government

Yes. The heptagonal helix is strong as a first model, but I would extend it into a seven-sector power phase screw. The key point is this: concentration of power should not be represented only as an angle, but also as distance from the center.

A normal heptagon has a problem: all seven corners are the same distance from the center. But “monocracy” and “polycentric democracy” are obviously not equally close to the center of power. For that reason, a simple heptagonal screw is geometrically somewhat dishonest.

Better:

Angle θ       = Which bearer of power dominates?
Radius r      = How concentrated or distributed is power?
Height z      = developmental stage: 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0 / ...
Color/density = rule of law, legitimacy, degree of violence, transparency

Formally:

Regime = (θ, r, z, q)

θ = one of 7 power sectors
r = power distribution from 0 to 1
z = organizational stage / historical version
q = quality of binding: law, control, publicity, legitimacy

So not only:

C7 × L3

but rather:

C7 × [0,1] × L3 × [0,1]

This is a heptagonal phase cylinder or a heptagonal power spiral in volume, not just a line on the surface.

1. The better form: a seven-sector power phase cylinder

Do not imagine a thin wire as a helix. Imagine a three-dimensional cylinder or cone divided into seven sectors.

                 z = developmental stage
                 ↑
                 │        3.0 digital / algorithmic
                 │       / 
                 │      /   2.0 bureaucratic / modern
                 │     /
                 │    /  1.0 personal / premodern
                 │
                 └────────────────────→ r = power distribution
                   center         edge
                concentration   distribution

The ground plan would look roughly like this:

                    6 Demos / Representation
                         ▲
             5 Law       │      7 Network / Federation
        Administration   │
                         │
  4 Elite / Property ◄───┼────►  1 Person / Sovereign
                         │
          3 Sacredness   │      2 Security / Coercive core
             Ideology    ▼

This is not yet a perfect picture, but the logic is clear:

  • Center = power is strongly bundled.
  • Edge = power is broadly distributed.
  • Sector = type of dominant bearer of power.
  • Height = historical / technical developmental stage.
  • Path through the space = history of a state or form of government.

This turns the heptagonal helix into a real phase space of government forms.

2. Why this extension is better

The old heptagonal helix had a hidden weakness: it placed forms of government into a fixed sequence:

Monocracy → cadre rule → oligarchy → mixed form → democracy → federation → network

That is didactically useful, but not fully clean logically.

Some categories are not the same type of category:

  • Monocracy describes concentration of power.
  • Oligarchy describes rule by a few.
  • Theocracy describes legitimation through religion.
  • Technocracy describes rule through expertise.
  • Federalism describes spatial distribution of power.
  • Democracy describes participation and authorization by the people.

These are different dimensions. If they are forced onto one circular line, precision is lost.

Therefore the better solution is:

Not: 7 names of government forms as corners
But: 7 bearers / media of power as sectors

3. I would redefine the 7 corners

The earlier seven-part order was usable. But for a genuinely strong model, I would choose the seven points differently: not as finished government forms, but as seven basic bearers of political power.

No. Power sector Basic question Typical forms
1 Person / sovereign Does a central person rule? Monarchy, tyranny, leader-state, personalist dictatorship
2 Violence / security Does the coercive apparatus rule? Military junta, security state, intelligence regime
3 Sacredness / ideology Does a doctrine of salvation, religion, or ideology rule? Theocracy, ideocracy, party doctrine, religious guardian state
4 Property / status / elite Do property, nobility, capital, or established elites rule? Aristocracy, oligarchy, plutocracy
5 Law / administration / expertise Do institutions, bureaucracy, law, or expertise rule? Bureaucracy, technocracy, nomocracy, administrative state
6 Demos / representation Do the people rule through elections, parliaments, parties? Republic, parliamentary democracy, mass democracy
7 Network / federation / commons Do many nodes, levels, and self-organizing units rule? Federation, council republic, communalism, liquid democracy, polycentric order

This is stronger than the old list because all seven categories answer the same question:

Where does the decisive bearer of power sit?

4. Dictatorship and democracy would then not be single corners

This is important.

In this model, dictatorship is not one point. Dictatorship is a region:

high concentration of power
+ weak control
+ weak public accountability
+ high coercive capacity

That region can lie in several sectors:

  • person sector → personal dictatorship
  • security sector → military dictatorship
  • ideology sector → totalitarian party dictatorship
  • administration sector → technocratic authoritarianism
  • property sector → oligarchic autocracy

Democracy is also not one corner, but a region:

broad distribution of power
+ public control
+ procedures
+ rights
+ accountability
+ participation

It typically lies more strongly in these sectors:

  • demos / representation
  • law / administration
  • network / federation

That makes the model much more realistic.

5. 1.0, 2.0, 3.0 in the improved model

The versions remain useful, but they do not mean “good” or “bad.” They designate the medium of organization.

1.0 = personal, premodern, local, tradition-bound power
2.0 = modern, bureaucratic, institutional, mass-societal power
3.0 = digital, data-based, networked, algorithmic power

The seven sectors then each receive their 1.0, 2.0, and 3.0 expression:

Power sector 1.0 2.0 3.0
Person king, tyrant, tribal leader leader-state, personalized party dictatorship platform autocrat, AI-supported cult of personality
Violence / security warrior caste, mercenary rule military junta, intelligence state predictive security state, digital control architecture
Sacredness / ideology priest-kingship, sacred monarchy theocracy, ideological party state algorithmic ideological steering, attention and truth regime
Property / elite nobility, landed property, patricians industrial, financial, media, and party elites platform, data, cloud, and infrastructure oligarchy
Law / administration scribal state, mandarinate, court administration modern administrative state, technocracy AI-supported administration, algorithmic governance
Demos / representation popular assembly, city republic, early parliament mass democracy, party and welfare state feedback democracy, digital deliberation, citizens’ assemblies plus platform procedures
Network / federation league of cities, tribal federation, village community federal state, associational state, strong civil society liquid democracy, polycentric platform federation, open protocol governance

Now you have not only 21 fields, but a much cleaner system:

7 power sectors × 3 developmental stages × variable power distribution × variable legal binding

6. Is the heptagon better than a circle?

Yes, but only for a specific purpose.

A circle is better when you want to show continuity:

Everything flows into everything else.
There are no hard borders.
History moves cyclically.

A heptagon is better when you want to distinguish types:

There are seven recognizable logics of power.
Each corner is an attractor.
Real systems lie between the corners.

The best compromise is therefore neither a hard heptagon nor a mere circle, but a:

soft heptagon

That means a circle or cylinder with seven attractor zones.

Not:

Type A ends here; type B begins there.

But:

Here one power logic condenses.
There another power logic condenses.
The spaces between them are mixed forms.

That is politically much more realistic. A state is rarely “only” an oligarchy or “only” a democracy. It is usually a mixture of person, apparatus, elite, law, demos, violence, and network.

7. Are 7 corners optimal?

Not objectively. But 7 is very defensible.

Optimality depends on what you want to optimize.

Number Effect
3 too coarse: one / few / many; good in the Aristotelian sense, but too poor for modern systems
4 elegant, but religion, military, bureaucracy, or network are usually swallowed
5 usable, but often still too narrow
6 strong for classical typologies, but modern platform, network, and technocratic forms remain blurry
7 good balance between clarity and differentiation
8–12 analytically more precise, but less elegant and harder to remember
20+ empirically more precise, but no longer an intuitive geometry

My judgment:

7 is not mathematically necessary,
but it is theoretically elegant and politically rich enough.

It is especially good if the seven corners are not arbitrary names of regimes, but seven sources of power.

The best seven-part list would therefore be:

1 Person
2 Violence / security
3 Sacredness / ideology
4 Property / elite
5 Law / administration / expertise
6 Demos / representation
7 Network / federation / commons

These seven are strong because they make almost every form of government describable as a mixture.

8. The completely different but very fitting form: a 7-source simplex

The best entirely different form is not a circle and not a heptagon, but a simplex.

If there are seven basic sources of political power, then every regime can be represented as a mixture of seven shares:

p1 = Person
p2 = Violence / security
p3 = Sacredness / ideology
p4 = Property / elite
p5 = Law / administration / expertise
p6 = Demos / representation
p7 = Network / federation / commons

With:

p1 + p2 + p3 + p4 + p5 + p6 + p7 = 1

Then every regime is a point in a 6-dimensional simplex:

Δ6 = { p ∈ R⁷ | pᵢ ≥ 0 and Σpᵢ = 1 }

That sounds abstract, but it is extremely fitting.

Example:

Personalist dictatorship:
Person 0.70
Violence 0.20
Ideology 0.05
Elite 0.03
Law 0.01
Demos 0.00
Network 0.01

Or:

Liberal mass democracy:
Person 0.05
Violence 0.05
Ideology 0.05
Elite 0.15
Law 0.25
Demos 0.35
Network 0.10

Or:

Cyber-oligarchy:
Person 0.05
Violence 0.10
Ideology 0.10
Elite 0.35
Law 0.10
Demos 0.05
Network 0.25

The simplex model is analytically stronger than the heptagon because it represents mixed forms naturally. The heptagon says:

This regime is close to corner 4.

The simplex says:

This regime consists of 35% elite rule,
25% administrative state,
20% network order,
10% security logic,
and so on.

That is much more precise.

9. Relationship between heptagon and simplex

The cleanest solution is:

Heptagon = intuitive projection
Simplex = mathematically deeper structure
Helix/cylinder = historical developmental form
Graph = transitions between states

Or as an overall model:

Form of government =
power mixture × power distribution × developmental stage × legal binding × time

Formally:

M = Δ6 × [0,1] × L × [0,1] × T

Where:

Δ6      = mixture of the seven sources of power
[0,1]   = concentration ↔ distribution
L       = 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0 / ...
[0,1]   = rule of law / legitimacy / transparency
T       = historical time

The history of a state would then not be a fixed category, but a curve through this space.

10. My final recommendation

For a book, diagram, or theory project, I would use three levels:

Level 1 — Intuitive symbol

Soft heptagon with seven power sectors

Good for readers, overview, typology.

Level 2 — Dynamic form

Heptagonal power phase cylinder with spiral trajectories

Good for history, 1.0/2.0/3.0, recurrence at a higher organizational level.

Level 3 — Mathematical deep model

7-source simplex plus time graph

Good for real analysis, mixed forms, and transitions.

The strongest formulation would be:

The heptagon is not the truth itself,
but the visible projection of a seven-pole power simplex.
The helix adds historical developmental stages.
The graph shows which transitions are actually possible or likely.

For your model: yes, probably — but only under one condition:

The seven corners must be the seven basic media of political power, not merely seven random forms of government.

Then 7 is strong.

Not:

Monarchy, oligarchy, democracy, federation ...

But:

Person
Violence
Sacredness/ideology
Property/elite
Law/administration
Demos
Network

That is much deeper.

Then almost all historical and contemporary forms of government can be understood as mixtures of these seven forces.

Heptagon better than circle?
For a typology: yes.
For a continuum: no.
For your project: best as a soft heptagon in a phase cylinder.

Are 7 corners optimal?
Not provably optimal, but very plausible: enough differentiation, still memorable, and strong enough to capture premodern, modern, and digital forms of government.

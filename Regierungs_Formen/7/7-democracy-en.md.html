7-Democracy: the optimal scale?

Yes — but only for this special form of 7-democracy, not for democracy in general.

The sharpened thesis is:

Ordinary democracy can work very well at smaller scales. But 7-democracy — democracy as a rotating order of power centers — probably requires a continental scale.

The “Asia-scale” is plausible here: Asia-Pacific had about 4.8 billion people in 2024, roughly 60% of the world’s population. That is exactly the zone where a democracy would no longer be merely a nation-state, but would still not be a world government.

The decisive point: not population, but the number of power centers

The best formulation is not:

Democracy works best at 4–5 billion people.

But:

7-democracy works best
when there is enough population, territory, and diversity
to produce many real power centers,
but not so many
that responsibility becomes impossible to locate.

So:

too small  = lack of power centers
optimal    = plurality of power centers
too large  = dissolution of responsibility

That is stronger than a mere claim about size.

Why smaller is not ideal for 7

Small democracies can be very stable, free, and prosperous. That is not the point.

The point is: they are often not 7, but rather 2, 3, or 5 with a democratic surface.

Small or medium-sized democracies more easily fall into simple axes:

government ↔ opposition
capital ↔ province
majority ↔ minority
elite ↔ people
city ↔ countryside

That is a 180° democracy.

Or they become a clean federation:

federal center ↔ states

That is closer to 3.

Or they become a centralized state with elections:

center ↓ administration ↓ citizens

That is democratized 5.

But 7 needs more: not only elections, not only federalism, not only separation of powers, but many non-identical power centers.

languages
regions
religious spaces
metropolises
coasts
interiors
industrial zones
agrarian zones
minorities
technology centers
generational interests

A small democracy can simulate this. A continental democracy has it for real.

Why larger is also not ideal

At world scale, or near-world scale, democracy flips into another problem:

Who is the people?
Which public sphere is shared?
Who can still hold whom accountable?
Where is exit?
Where is opposition?
Where is external correction?
Who controls the global control authority?

If the system becomes too large, it does not create better democracy, but something closer to:

world administration
technocracy
symbolic world parliament
imperial rhetoric of humanity
global protocol order

Then we are no longer at 7, but closer to a later form:

8 = planetary protocol order / civilizational administration / world interface

7 still needs conflict, addressability, counter-centers, constituencies, publics, scandal, and the possibility of removal.

Too large means:

Power is everywhere,
so responsibility is nowhere.

Why the Asia-scale is plausible

Asia-scale is politically interesting because it satisfies three conditions at once.

1. Enough internal strangeness

A 7-democracy must not consist of an almost homogeneous people.

It needs real differences:

multiple language families
multiple civilizational spaces
multiple climate zones
multiple economic systems
multiple historical memories
multiple religious spaces
multiple metropolitan axes

Otherwise nothing rotates. One axis wins.

2. Enough power centers against centralization

At Asia-scale, a single center cannot absorb everything if the constitution is well designed.

There are too many counter-poles:

north
south
coast
interior
megacities
resource zones
technology zones
agrarian zones
border zones
highlands
island spaces

That is the anti-imperial function of size.

One center can dominate a small democracy.
One center can dominate a continental 7-democracy only
if the architecture has already failed.

3. Still not the whole world

This matters.

A 7-democracy still needs an outside:

diplomacy
comparison
migration
trade
competition
norm correction
exit threat
external mirror

If it encompasses the whole world, it loses that mirror. Then it becomes metaphysical: “humanity decides.” That sounds noble, but institutionally it is dangerous.

India is the real test case — but not the finished 7

India shows that democracy at enormous population scale is not impossible. The Election Commission of India administers elections to the Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and the offices of President and Vice-President; that is evidence that mass democracy can be technically and organizationally scaled.

But India also shows the danger: International IDEA currently describes India as mid-range in its democracy categories and notes declines in areas such as Access to Justice, Civic Engagement, Credible Elections, Free Political Parties, and Judicial Independence.

So:

Large democracy is possible.
But size alone does not produce 7.

India is closer to:

large election machine
+ federalism
+ strong centralization tendencies
+ cultural dominance conflicts

So it is not automatically 7. It is an indication of what 7 would have to solve.

The hard theoretical core

There is an interesting tension in the research. The classical assumption often was that smaller polities are more democracy-friendly because participation can be more direct. Gerring et al. argue, for the modern era, that larger populations can also bring democratic advantages, partly because power has to be more institutionalized and territorially distributed.

That fits this thesis exactly.

But current democracy data warn against naïveté: V-Dem reports in its Democracy Report 2026 that by the end of 2025, 74% of the world population lived in autocracies and only 7% lived in liberal democracies. Size therefore does not automatically correlate with democratic quality.

The conclusion:

Size is not the cause.
Size is raw material.

The architecture decides
whether size becomes democracy,
empire,
centralized state,
or chaos.

As an angle model

Too small

       ↑
       |
       |
Center / Majority
       |
       ↓

Problem:

too few counter-angles
too simple an opposition
too easy an elite concentration

This often becomes:

2 = government/opposition
3 = federation
5 = centralized state with elections

Asia-scale / 7

              ↑ Future
        ↗               ↖
   Law                   Regions

→ Citizens     ○          Minorities ←

   Commune              Executive
        ↘               ↙
              ↓ Feedback

Here power is not erased, but forced to rotate.

No center is final.
No majority is final.
No region is final.
No court is final.
No expertocracy is final.
No minority veto is final.

That is 7.

Too large / world scale

↗ ↑ ↖ ← ↙ ↓ ↘ → ↗ ↑ ↖ ← ↙ ↓ ↘ ...

Problem:

too many directions
too little addressability
too little shared public sphere
too little removability
too little external correction

Then democracy becomes the administration of complexity, not rule through legitimized feedback.

The most precise answer

Yes, it may be true that the best scale for this special form of democracy is roughly a continental polity with an Asia-sized population.

But not because 4.8 billion people are magically democratic.

Rather, because this scale probably maximizes:

diversity of power centers
+
institutional reciprocity
+
shared accountability

The formula:

below that:
democracy can be good,
but it is usually not 7.

at Asia-scale:
7 becomes plausible,
because many power centers really exist
and still fit into one constitutional order.

above that:
7 dissolves into world administration,
technocracy, or protocol order.

The best sentence:

7-democracy is probably the largest democratic form before world government — and the smallest form in which central rule becomes structurally implausible.

Even shorter:

democracy in general:
can work at smaller scales too.

7-democracy:
needs continental scale.

world government:
is already no longer 7.

So yes: as an architectural thesis, this is highly plausible. Not as an empirical law. But as political geometry, it is strong.
